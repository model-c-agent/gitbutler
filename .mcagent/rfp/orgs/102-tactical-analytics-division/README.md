# Tactical Analytics Division

**"Every play is a maneuver. Every game is a campaign. Every season is a war."**

---

## Mission Briefing

The Tactical Analytics Division (TAD) applies military operational analysis to professional sports. Founded in 2020 by Colonel (Ret.) James Harding and Dr. Sarah Okonkwo, the Division operates from a converted signals building at a decommissioned RAF base near Cambridge, England.

Colonel Harding spent 28 years in British Army intelligence, specializing in pattern recognition for counter-IED operations in Afghanistan. Dr. Okonkwo holds a PhD in operations research from MIT and spent five years at RAND Corporation analyzing logistics optimization for the US Department of Defense. They met at a NATO analytics conference in 2019, discovered a shared obsession with football tactics, and realized that military operational analysis techniques -- originally developed for battlefield pattern recognition -- could be directly applied to sports formation analysis.

The Division employs eight analysts, all with military or defense research backgrounds. They sell tactical analysis packages to professional football clubs in the Premier League and Championship. Their product: pre-match tactical briefings that analyze the opposing team's formations, transition patterns, and set-piece tendencies with the same rigor applied to military threat assessments.

## Operational Approach

The Division's analytical framework treats a football pitch as a battlespace. Formations are dispositions. Player movements are maneuvers. Transitions are operations. Set pieces are planned assaults. This is not metaphor -- it is a direct application of established military analytical frameworks to a different domain.

Their software stack reflects military discipline: classified (client-proprietary) and unclassified (open-source tools) codebases are strictly separated. All analysis is version-controlled. All outputs are signed. Chain of custody for client data is documented and auditable.

GitButler's virtual branches map to their existing practice of maintaining separate analytical tracks for different tactical scenarios. The `but-ai` plugin would automate the data preparation and initial pattern recognition phases, allowing analysts to focus on the high-level tactical interpretation that requires human judgment.

## Philosophy

War is decided by the quality of intelligence, not the size of the army. Similarly, matches are decided by tactical preparation, not just player talent. The Division exists to provide intelligence that turns marginal advantages into decisive ones.

They apply the same principle to AI agents: an agent provides intelligence (data analysis, pattern recognition, automated preparation). It does not make decisions. Decisions are made by commanders (head coaches) based on intelligence briefings (tactical reports) prepared by analysts (the Division).

## Internal Tension

**The Automation Boundary.** Major (Ret.) Chen Wei wants to automate opponent modeling: feed game footage into a vision model, extract formation data, generate tactical reports without human analysis. Colonel Harding insists on human analysts in the loop for all tactical conclusions. "A machine can tell you where the players are. It cannot tell you why the manager put them there." The debate is ongoing; Chen's prototype exists but is not deployed to clients.

## Notable Achievement

The Division's tactical briefing for a Championship club's 2025 playoff semi-final identified a specific vulnerability in the opposing team's high press: a gap that opened between the left centre-back and left-back during the opposing team's pressing triggers. The club exploited this gap three times in the first half, scoring twice. They won the match 3-1 and were promoted. The club's manager credited "exceptional preparation" in the post-match press conference without naming the Division, which is how the Division prefers it.
