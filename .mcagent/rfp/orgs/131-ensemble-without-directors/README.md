# Ensemble Without Directors

*Every decision made by full ensemble deliberation. Every show opened on time.*

---

## How We Work

Ensemble Without Directors is a theater company based in Portland, Oregon, that has operated without a director, artistic director, managing director, or any other kind of director since its founding in 2014. Every creative and administrative decision — from casting to set design to ticket pricing to what brand of gaffer tape to buy — is made by full ensemble deliberation.

The company was founded by nine theater artists who had worked together at the Portland Repertory Theatre and shared a single frustration: the director always won. In disagreements about interpretation, staging, design, or pacing, the director's vision prevailed. This is standard practice in theater. The Ensemble considers it a structural defect.

"A play is not a director's vision," says Soo-jin Park, one of the founding members. "A play is what happens between the actors and the audience. The director is not in that space. The actors are."

The Ensemble currently has twelve members. All decisions require a two-thirds majority. Abstentions count as abstentions, not as votes against. Deliberation has no time limit, but a member may call for a "curtain vote" — a demand for immediate resolution — if deliberation has exceeded 90 minutes. Curtain votes require unanimous agreement to proceed. If even one member objects, deliberation continues.

The company produces three shows per year. Despite the deliberative process, they have never missed an opening night. Soo-jin attributes this to "the deadline of the audience" — when 200 people have bought tickets for Friday at 8pm, deliberation acquires urgency.

## Philosophy

### 1. No Voice Is Louder Than Another

In ensemble deliberation, every member speaks. Not "has the opportunity to speak" — every member *speaks*. Silence is treated as suppressed dissent. If a member is quiet during deliberation, the facilitator (a rotating role) will ask them directly: "What do you think?" This is not politeness. It is protocol. The Ensemble has learned that the member who stays quiet is often the one who sees the problem no one else does.

### 2. Consensus Is Not Unanimity

The Ensemble requires two-thirds, not unanimity. They tried unanimity for the first year and discovered that it gave every member an effective veto, which produced either paralysis or resentment. Two-thirds allows strong dissent to be recorded without blocking action. Dissenting members may file a "counter-note" — a written objection that is preserved in the company's deliberation archive.

### 3. The Show Is the Test

No amount of deliberation can predict whether a show will work. The audience is the test. The Ensemble treats opening night as a deployment — the moment when all the deliberation resolves into observable reality. Post-opening retrospectives are mandatory and often more heated than pre-production deliberations.

## Internal Tensions

**Speed versus inclusion.** The deliberative process takes time. Production timelines do not expand to accommodate deliberation. The tension is permanent and productive: the company must find ways to make good decisions quickly without excluding voices. They have experimented with sub-committees (rejected: "creates hierarchy"), delegated decisions (rejected: "creates directors"), and asynchronous deliberation via shared documents (accepted reluctantly, used mainly for administrative decisions). The tension has never been resolved. Several members believe it should not be.

## Achievement

In 2025, the Ensemble's production of Caryl Churchill's *Love and Information* was selected for the Edinburgh Festival Fringe. The production used a unique staging approach developed through seven weeks of deliberation: every scene was staged by a different pair of ensemble members, with no single staging authority. Critics described it as "kaleidoscopic" and "either brilliant or chaotic, possibly both." It sold out twelve performances.
