# Harvest Command Authority

**"Precision agriculture demands precision engineering. No exceptions."**

---

## Mission Statement

Harvest Command Authority (HCA) is a private agricultural technology consultancy that applies military operational methodology to large-scale precision agriculture. Founded in 2019 by Col. (Ret.) Frank Dunbar and Lt. Col. (Ret.) Priya Mehta, HCA provides autonomous harvesting systems, sensor-fused field mapping, and logistics optimization for commercial farming operations across the American Midwest and Australian grain belt.

The company employs 28 staff organized into four sections: Intelligence (field sensing and data analysis), Operations (autonomous vehicle deployment), Logistics (post-harvest transport optimization), and Engineering (systems development). The organizational chart is not a suggestion -- it is the chain of command. Section heads report to Mehta. Mehta reports to Dunbar. Dunbar reports to the client.

## Origins

Dunbar commanded an Army logistics battalion before retiring. He spent twenty years ensuring that ammunition, fuel, and food reached forward operating bases in contested terrain on schedule, every time. When he retired and bought a 2,000-acre wheat farm in Kansas, he was appalled by the agricultural industry's tolerance for waste: combines running inefficient patterns, grain trucks waiting hours for loading, sensor data collected but never operationalized.

He called Mehta, his former S-3 (operations officer), and proposed applying combined arms planning methodology to farming. Mehta, who had been working at a defense contractor and hating it, agreed immediately. They wrote their first operational plan for a wheat harvest the way they would write a plan for a logistics convoy: with phase lines, decision points, and contingency branches.

It worked. Their first client -- Dunbar's own farm -- saw a 19% reduction in harvest time and a 12% reduction in fuel costs. Word spread through the farming community, and HCA now serves 14 farms across three states and two countries.

## Why This RFP

HCA's autonomous systems generate thousands of configuration changes, sensor calibrations, and route optimizations per season. These changes are currently tracked in a combination of Git repositories and Excel spreadsheets that Dunbar describes as "an intelligence failure waiting to happen." The `but-ai` plugin offers a path to unified version control for autonomous agricultural agents with proper attribution and audit trails.

## Internal Tension

Okoye runs Engineering like a military staff section: morning briefings, tasking orders, after-action reviews. Ana Petrov, who has never served in the military and was hired for her expertise in distributed systems, finds the command structure stifling. She has proposed, three times, that Engineering adopt a flatter structure for software development while maintaining military discipline for field operations. Okoye has denied the proposal each time, citing "unity of command." Petrov stays because the technical problems are interesting and the pay is good. She has started referring to stand-up meetings as "formation" without irony, which worries her.

## Notable Achievement

During the 2025 Australian wheat harvest, HCA deployed 8 autonomous combines across a 5,000-acre operation. The system executed 4,200 route adjustments in real time based on sensor data, weather updates, and grain moisture readings. Total harvest time: 72 hours, versus the client's historical average of 120 hours. Zero equipment collisions. Zero grain spoilage. Dunbar called it "the cleanest operation since Desert Storm." The client called it "the best investment we ever made."
