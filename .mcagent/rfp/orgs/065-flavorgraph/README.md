# FlavorGraph

**"Strawberry and basil. Coffee and blue cheese. The graph knows."**

---

## Origin

FlavorGraph was founded in 2023 by Dr. Priya Chakraborty, a computational chemist who spent her postdoc at the MIT Media Lab building molecular models of flavor compounds, and Ray Xu, a former Doordash engineer who was tired of recommendation algorithms that suggested pizza to people who just ordered pizza.

Priya's research focused on a simple observation: flavor compatibility between ingredients is governed by shared volatile compounds. Strawberry and basil pair well because they share over 20 aromatic compounds. Coffee and blue cheese pair well because they share 2-methylbutyric acid. These chemical relationships form a graph — a network where ingredients are nodes and shared compounds are edges. Navigate the graph, and you can predict which ingredient combinations will taste good before anyone puts them in a mouth.

Ray saw the business case immediately. The global food industry spends billions on product development, most of it through trial-and-error. What if you could computationally screen flavor pairings before committing to a test kitchen? What if a restaurant could generate novel menu items by traversing the graph for pairings that are chemically compatible but culinarily unexplored?

FlavorGraph raised a seed round, hired three engineers, and built a graph neural network trained on 1,200 flavor compounds, 8,000 ingredients, and 400,000 known pairings from global cuisines. The model predicts novel pairings with a 73% acceptance rate in blind taste tests (human baseline for predicting novel pairings: ~45%).

## Philosophy

Data reveals what intuition cannot. A chef with 30 years of experience knows thousands of pairings from memory. FlavorGraph's graph knows millions. The value is not in replacing the chef's intuition — it is in extending it into territory the chef has not explored. The best pairings are the ones that surprise: they are not in any cookbook, they have no cultural precedent, but they work because the chemistry works.

They approach AI agents the same way: the value of an agent is not in reproducing what a human would do — it is in exploring code-space territory the human has not considered. An agent that generates the same patch a human would have written is useful but not exceptional. An agent that generates a correct patch using an approach the human had not considered is where the real value lies.

## Internal Tension

Priya and Ray disagree about transparency. Priya wants the graph and the model to be fully open — publish the weights, open-source the training data, let anyone reproduce the results. She is an academic; she believes in reproducibility. Ray wants the model proprietary — it is the company's moat. The compromise: the graph structure (ingredients and compound relationships) is open-source. The neural network weights (which predict novel pairings) are proprietary. Priya considers this "publishing the map but hiding the compass." Ray considers it "good business."

## Achievement

In October 2025, FlavorGraph's model predicted that miso paste and dark chocolate would create a compelling umami-bitter pairing in a dessert context. A Michelin-starred chef in Copenhagen tested the pairing in a tasting menu. The dish — a miso-chocolate mousse with black sesame — became the restaurant's most-requested dessert and was featured in Bon Appetit's "Dishes of the Year." The chef credited FlavorGraph. Priya was thrilled. Ray calculated the PR value.

## Signature Quirk

Every commit message includes a flavor pairing generated by the model for that commit's hash. The hash is used as a seed to sample the graph, producing a unique pairing. Example: `feat: add compound similarity endpoint [Pairing: smoked paprika + white peach (87% confidence)]`. The pairings are sometimes delicious. They are sometimes disgusting. The team has tried all of them.
