# Ledger Liberation Front

**"Your books are cooked. We brought the fire extinguisher."**

## What We Are

The Ledger Liberation Front is an anarchist collective of forensic accountants, data analysts, and whistleblower advocates who conduct pro bono corporate fraud investigations. We were founded in 2018 by three people who were tired of watching white-collar criminals get away with it because the victims could not afford forensic auditors.

Our founding principle: forensic accounting should not be a luxury. When a nonprofit discovers its treasurer has been embezzling, when a union finds its pension fund has been looted, when a small business realizes its partner has been siphoning revenue -- these people need forensic accountants, and forensic accountants charge $400/hour. We charge nothing.

We have investigated 47 cases since 2018. We have helped recover $14 million. We have never accepted a fee, a donation tied to a case, or a favor. We are funded by a mix of foundation grants, individual donations, and the occasional cryptocurrency transfer from someone who prefers not to be identified.

## How We Got Here

The collective started when Vero, Kash, and June met at a Chaos Communication Congress workshop on financial transparency. Vero was a forensic accountant who had just been fired for refusing to sign off on an audit she believed was incomplete. Kash was a data scientist who had built leaked-data analysis tools for investigative journalists. June was a labor organizer who had watched two union pension funds get gutted by management while auditors looked the other way.

They decided to build an organization that would do what auditors refused to: follow the money, regardless of where it led, on behalf of people who could not pay to have it followed.

The software pivot happened in 2023, when the collective was drowning in case data. Forty-seven cases, each involving thousands of transactions, stored in a mix of spreadsheets, PDFs, and scanned bank statements. Kash built an agent pipeline that ingested transaction data, identified anomalous patterns (round-number transactions, unusual timing, transactions just below reporting thresholds), and generated investigation leads.

The pipeline used Git for version control because Kash insisted that every analytical step be reproducible and auditable. "If we go to court with this, we need to show our work. Every step. Every assumption. Every branch not taken."

## Philosophy

### On Power

Fraud is a power crime. It is committed by people who have enough institutional authority to manipulate records, enough social capital to avoid scrutiny, and enough money to hire lawyers when scrutiny arrives. Fighting fraud requires tools that equalize the power imbalance. Free forensic accounting is one such tool. Open-source investigation software is another.

### On Transparency

We publish our methodologies. We open-source our tools. We document our analytical steps in Git so that anyone can audit our audit. If our analysis is wrong, we want people to be able to find where we went wrong and correct it. Opacity is the enemy -- in corporate books and in our own work.

### On AI

AI agents are force multipliers for investigation. A human analyst can review 500 transactions per day. An AI agent can flag anomalies in 50,000. We use agents for the grunt work -- pattern detection, threshold analysis, timeline construction -- and reserve human judgment for the interpretation. An agent can tell you that 200 transactions were for exactly $9,999 (just below the $10,000 reporting threshold). A human can tell you what that means.

## Tension

**The Evidence Standard.** Vero insists that every finding produced by an AI agent be verified by a human before it appears in a case report. She has seen too many investigations fail because the evidence was generated by tools that the defense could discredit. June argues that requiring human verification of every agent finding creates a bottleneck that lets statutes of limitation expire. "We are not a court. We are investigators. Our standard should be 'credible lead,' not 'beyond reasonable doubt.'" The current compromise: agent findings marked as "AI-generated, human-verified" or "AI-generated, unverified" with the distinction clearly noted in all reports.

## Achievement

In 2025, the collective completed its largest investigation: a two-year probe into a community development financial institution that had diverted $8.2 million in federal grant money to shell companies controlled by the institution's CEO. The investigation reconstructed 12,000 transactions across 7 bank accounts and 3 shell companies, producing a 340-page report that was submitted to the Department of Justice. The CEO was indicted. The case is pending trial. The collective's agent pipeline generated 60% of the transaction timeline, which Vero then verified by hand over four months.

## Working Style

Cases drive everything. No case, no work. When a new case arrives, the collective meets (signal group chat, not voice) to assess scope and assign leads. Vero leads the investigation. Kash builds the data pipeline. June coordinates with the client. Sol ensures the evidence chain is unbroken.

All work happens on case-specific branches. No cross-case contamination. When a case closes, the branch is archived (not deleted -- evidence must be preserved).
