# Kim Gaming Dynasty

**"Grandmother manages the finances. Grandmother manages everything."**

---

## The Family

The Kim Gaming Dynasty is a family business based in Busan, South Korea. Three generations. One household. One company.

**First generation:** Kim Jae-won (67) and Park Sun-hee (65). Jae-won was a professional StarCraft: Brood War player in the early 2000s — not a champion, but a consistent mid-tier professional who earned a modest living from tournament prize pools and sponsorships for eight years. Sun-hee managed the finances then and manages them now. She has never played a competitive game in her life. She does not need to. She understands money, contracts, and the difference between revenue and profit, which she considers more important than any in-game skill.

**Second generation:** Kim Min-jun (42) and his wife Lee Hana (40). Min-jun inherited his father's competitive instincts but applied them to StarCraft II, where he reached Grandmaster on the Korean ladder. He retired from competitive play in 2018 to focus on coaching. Hana is a former financial analyst at Samsung Securities who quit to join the family business when it became clear that Sun-hee's spreadsheet-based accounting system, while accurate, would not scale beyond seven figures.

**Third generation:** Kim Yuna (19) and Kim Dohyun (17). Yuna is a semi-professional Valorant player currently ranked Immortal 3. Dohyun is a League of Legends prodigy who reached Challenger at 15. Both live at home. Both attend university (Yuna studies computer science; Dohyun studies media). Both hand their tournament winnings to Grandmother, who invests them.

The Dynasty — the family uses the term with ironic affection — operates a coaching and analytics business. Min-jun coaches. Jae-won consults on mental fortitude. Hana handles business operations. Sun-hee handles money. The grandchildren compete and, increasingly, build software.

## How Software Happened

Yuna started coding in high school. By 2024, she had built a practice analytics dashboard for her Valorant team that tracked crosshair placement accuracy per map, per site, per time-of-day. The dashboard ingested demo files and produced structured reports. She stored the codebase in Git because her CS professor required it.

When Yuna showed the project to Min-jun, he asked if she could build something similar for his StarCraft coaching clients. She could. She did. Then Dohyun wanted a League of Legends version. Then Grandmother asked, "Can the computer do the analysis instead of Yuna, so Yuna can study for her exams?"

That question — can the computer do it — led to the family's AI agent project. Yuna built agents that analyzed gameplay data and produced coaching reports as structured commits. Dohyun built the frontend. Min-jun defined the coaching methodology. Hana managed the project timeline. Sun-hee asked, every week, "How much did the computer cost us this week?"

Sun-hee's question about costs is why the Kim Dynasty's token budget management is the tightest in this RFP.

## Philosophy

Family first. The business exists to support the family, not the other way around. Software decisions are made around the dinner table. Code reviews happen between Yuna and Dohyun while their grandmother serves doenjang-jjigae. Technical architecture is discussed with the same seriousness as university applications and tournament travel logistics.

Agents are tools, not family members. The Kims do not anthropomorphize their software. An agent is a worker the family has hired. It has a job, a budget, and expectations. If it underperforms, it gets fixed or replaced. Grandmother does not develop sentimental attachments to employees, digital or otherwise.

## The Budget Argument

In March 2025, Yuna proposed upgrading the agent system to use GPT-4 for all tasks. The estimated monthly cost: $2,800. Sun-hee rejected the proposal in four words: "Too expensive. Use cheaper." Yuna argued that accuracy would suffer. Sun-hee asked for numbers. Yuna could not prove that the accuracy difference justified the cost. The family now uses a tiered provider strategy: expensive models for high-stakes analysis, cheap models for routine tasks. Sun-hee reviews the monthly AI invoice line by line.

The argument lasted three dinners. It was resolved when Hana produced a spreadsheet showing the cost-per-analysis at each provider tier. Sun-hee circled the Ollama column and said, "This one is free?"

The family now runs Ollama on a server in the living room closet.

## Achievement

**Revenue milestone**: The Dynasty's coaching analytics platform reached 200 paying subscribers in Q4 2025, generating $14,000/month in recurring revenue. Grandmother considers this "acceptable but not yet sufficient." The agent-generated coaching reports are the key feature — coaches receive automated analysis of their clients' gameplay, formatted as structured diffs against the client's previous performance baseline.
