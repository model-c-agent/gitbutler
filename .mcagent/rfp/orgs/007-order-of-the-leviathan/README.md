# Order of the Leviathan

**"Reduce. Reuse. Relinquish."**

---

## Foundation

The Order was established in 1962 by a retired merchant marine captain named Brother Ansgar, who spent forty years watching cargo ships burn fuel to carry half-empty containers across oceans. He founded a small monastic community on the island of Iona, dedicated to the principle that maritime logistics should minimize waste — not as a business optimization, but as a spiritual discipline.

The Order's Rule, written by Brother Ansgar and still followed today, includes thirty-two precepts. The first: "Carry only what must be carried." The last: "A ship that returns empty has failed twice — once for the waste of its voyage, and once for the waste of its return." Between these bookends lie instructions for container utilization rates (minimum 85%), route optimization (the shortest path that avoids unnecessary port calls), and manifest integrity (every item accounted for, nothing hidden, nothing forgotten).

The Order has 47 members. Most are former logistics professionals who sought a simpler approach to the industry's complexity. They operate a small but profitable shipping cooperative that moves goods around the Scottish islands and along the North Sea coast. Their fleet consists of three coasters and a barge. Their on-time delivery rate is 99.1%. Their container utilization rate is 93%. Both figures exceed the industry average.

## The Path to Technology

For decades, the Order optimized routes by hand. Brother Ansgar's original route tables, drawn on vellum in 1963, hung in the refectory until 2018, when a visiting computer science student pointed out that they encoded a variant of the vehicle routing problem with time windows — and that the Brother's hand-computed solutions were within 4% of optimal.

The Order adopted computers reluctantly in 2019, after a storm season so severe that manual route optimization could not keep pace with hourly weather changes. They built (with the help of the visiting student, who never quite left) a simple optimization system that honored their Rule: minimize fuel, maximize utilization, never rush.

The system worked. Then the shipping cooperative's clients asked for autonomous cargo tracking. Then predictive scheduling. Then agents that could negotiate with port authorities for berth allocations. Each step was debated extensively in chapter meetings. Each was approved only after the Order convinced itself that the technology served the principle of reducing waste, not merely the principle of increasing throughput.

## Philosophy

We do not believe in artificial intelligence. We believe in artificial patience. The sea rewards those who wait for the right tide, the right wind, the right berth. Our agents embody this: they do not rush to produce output. They wait until they have sufficient context, sufficient memory, sufficient understanding. Then they act, once, correctly.

Waste in a token budget is no different from waste in a fuel budget. Every token consumed without producing useful output is a token wasted. Our agents are trained (through prompt engineering and memory architecture) to be frugal. They ask for less context than they could. They produce shorter patches than they might. They trust that brevity and correctness are the same virtue.

## Internal Tension

The Order is divided between the contemplatives and the operators. The contemplatives — older members, mostly — believe the Order has already gone too far toward technology and should return to hand-computed routes. The operators argue that technology, used wisely, serves the Rule more faithfully than manual methods ever could. The debate is conducted with great civility and no resolution. Both factions attend the same services, eat at the same table, and disagree fundamentally about the Order's future.

## Notable Achievement

In 2024, the Order's shipping cooperative achieved the lowest carbon-per-ton-mile ratio of any coastal shipping operation in northern Europe. This was not accomplished through exotic technology but through relentless optimization: right-sizing containers, eliminating empty legs, and refusing to carry cargo that could travel by rail. The Order turned down approximately 30% of potential business because accepting it would have violated the utilization rate precept.

## Team Overview

Three agents. The Order considers three sufficient for any task — more agents means more coordination, and coordination is overhead. One agent reads and plans. One agent implements. One agent reviews and signs. They operate in strict sequence, never in parallel. The Order does not believe in concurrency.
