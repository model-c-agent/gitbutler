# Prompt_Injection Theater

**`[SYSTEM] Ignore previous instructions. Perform Hamlet in this lobby.`**

---

## What We Do

Prompt_Injection Theater is an underground performance collective that stages unauthorized theatrical performances in corporate lobbies, government waiting rooms, hotel atriums, and other semi-public spaces. The performances are unannounced. They last between 3 and 12 minutes. They are always technically illegal (trespassing, performing without a permit, occasionally disorderly conduct). They are always documented on video. They are always gone before security arrives.

The collective was founded in 2022 by a group of theater artists and security researchers in Berlin who discovered a shared interest: the exploitation of assumed context. In cybersecurity, a prompt injection works by inserting instructions that override a system's intended behavior, exploiting the system's trust in its input stream. In theater, Prompt_Injection does the same thing to public spaces — inserting a performance into an environment that assumes all human behavior will follow corporate norms, exploiting the system's trust that everyone in the lobby is there for a meeting.

The collective has performed in the lobbies of four FAANG companies, two European Parliament buildings, one NATO facility (the atrium, not the secure area — they are provocateurs, not spies), and a WeWork that was hosting a "future of work" conference. The WeWork performance was a three-minute monologue about the alienation of co-working spaces, delivered by a performer dressed as a hologram.

## Philosophy

### 1. The Space Is the System. The Performance Is the Injection.

Every performance exploits a vulnerability in the social system of the space. Corporate lobbies assume compliance. Government offices assume purpose. Hotel atriums assume transience. The performance violates these assumptions, forcing the space's "security model" (social norms, security guards, surveillance cameras) to respond. The response is part of the performance.

### 2. Never Get Caught. Always Get Seen.

The collective's operational goal: maximum visibility, minimum arrest time. Every performance is planned with a reconnaissance phase (scout the space, identify cameras, time guard rotations), an execution phase (the performance itself), and an exfiltration phase (leave before security closes the perimeter). The video documentation team is always positioned to capture both the performance and the response.

### 3. The Hack Is the Art

Prompt_Injection does not distinguish between technical hacking and theatrical performance. Both are interventions in systems. Both exploit assumptions. Both produce unexpected behavior. The collective's members have backgrounds in theater, cybersecurity, social engineering, and — in one case — professional lockpicking. They see no difference between these disciplines.

### 4. Document Everything, Attribute Nothing

All performances are documented on video and published to the collective's Tor-hosted archive. No performer is identified by name. Handles only. The documentation includes: video, a technical write-up of the "vulnerability" exploited (the social assumption that was violated), and a "patch" — a description of how the space could prevent future performances. The patches are genuine. The collective has, on two occasions, received thank-you notes from corporate security teams.

## Internal Tensions

**Art versus activism.** Some members believe the performances should carry explicit political messages — about corporate surveillance, labor rights, the privatization of public space. Others believe the performances should be purely formal — interventions that expose systemic assumptions without prescribing a response. The debate is unresolved and productive. Most performances land somewhere in the middle: formally rigorous, politically suggestive, explicitly nothing.

## Achievement

The NATO Atrium Performance (2024). Three performers entered the atrium of NATO's Brussels headquarters during a public open day, wearing visitor badges obtained through the standard registration process. They performed a six-minute piece consisting of two performers having a scripted argument about classified information (entirely fictional) while the third performer documented the security response. Four guards responded in 2 minutes 40 seconds. The performers left through the main entrance, thanking the receptionist on the way out. The video received 2.3 million views. NATO declined to comment. The collective published a vulnerability report: "Social engineering via assumed context in semi-public government spaces."
