# Information Operations Library

**"Reference delivery within 90 seconds. Combat-effective knowledge."**

---

## The Library

The Information Operations Library (IOL) is a specialized military library attached to the United States Army's Information Operations Center at Fort Liberty, North Carolina. It is staffed by reference librarians who hold security clearances and have been trained in tactical information delivery — the discipline of getting the right information to the right person in the right format within the time window where that information can affect a decision.

The IOL was established in 2008, when Colonel (Ret.) Sandra Kwan recognized that information operations units were drowning in data and starving for knowledge. The units had access to intelligence databases, open-source feeds, social media monitoring tools, and classified reports — terabytes of data, none of it organized for rapid retrieval by a field officer who needed a specific answer in under two minutes.

Kwan, who had a Master of Library Science from the University of North Carolina before her military career, proposed a radical solution: embed professional reference librarians in information operations units. Not intelligence analysts. Not data scientists. Librarians — people trained in the art of understanding a question, navigating an information system, and delivering a precise answer. The Army approved the pilot. The pilot succeeded. The IOL has been operational for 18 years.

The IOL's reference librarians are not typical military personnel. They wear uniforms but speak in the vocabulary of library science. They file reports using a classification scheme derived from the Library of Congress system. They maintain a physical reference collection — 4,000 volumes of regional studies, language dictionaries, cultural guides, and doctrinal manuals — alongside their digital holdings. They call their workstations "reference desks."

## Philosophy

### 1. The Reference Interview Is the Most Important Step

Before retrieving information, the librarian conducts a reference interview: a structured conversation to determine what the patron (the requesting officer) actually needs, as opposed to what they asked for. Officers often ask for data ("Give me the population of Kandahar province") when they need analysis ("Is this village growing or shrinking, and why?"). The reference interview translates the question from the patron's frame to the information system's frame.

### 2. Speed Without Accuracy Is Misinformation

The IOL operates under time pressure. Field requests carry deadlines measured in minutes. But a fast answer that is wrong is worse than no answer — it creates false confidence. The IOL's standard: deliver within 90 seconds, but never guess. If the answer is not available within the time window, the response is "Answer not available within time window. Extended research initiated. Estimated delivery: [time]."

### 3. Classification Is a Weapon

Information that is correctly classified — organized, indexed, cross-referenced, and filed — is a force multiplier. Information that is misclassified is a liability. The IOL maintains its own classification scheme, adapted from the Library of Congress system for military information operations. Every document, every report, every retrieved answer is classified before filing. The classification step adds 15 seconds to every transaction. The IOL considers this the best 15 seconds in their workflow.

### 4. The Stacks Are Never Closed

The IOL operates 24/7 in three shifts. Information operations do not pause for library hours.

## Internal Tensions

**Military discipline versus librarian culture.** Military units operate on orders. Libraries operate on inquiry. When a colonel asks the IOL for information, the librarian is trained to ask clarifying questions — which, in military culture, can be perceived as questioning the order. The IOL has developed a protocol for this: clarifying questions are framed as "confirmation of requirements" rather than challenges. "Sir, confirming: you need the current population, or the pre-conflict population? Both are available." This works most of the time. Colonel Kwan mediates the rest.

## Achievement

During a 2023 exercise at the Joint Readiness Training Center, the IOL's embedded reference team delivered 847 reference responses over 72 hours, with a median delivery time of 67 seconds and an accuracy rate of 98.2% (verified by independent review). The exercise after-action report described the IOL as "the single highest-value information asset in the brigade."
