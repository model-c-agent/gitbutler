# The Dramaturgy & Cognition Lab

*Measuring what happens between the stage and the brain.*

---

## The Lab

The Dramaturgy & Cognition Lab (DCL) is an interdisciplinary research group at the University of Amsterdam, jointly housed between the Faculty of Humanities (Theater Studies) and the Faculty of Science (Cognitive Neuroscience). The lab studies what happens in the audience's brain during a theatrical performance — and, more specifically, what structural features of the dramaturgy (the composition and arrangement of the theatrical text) correlate with measurable neural responses.

The lab was founded in 2018 by Dr. Lena Scholz, a dramaturg turned cognitive scientist. Scholz had spent fifteen years as a working dramaturg in German-language theater — at the Schaubuehne, the Burgtheater, and the Thalia Theater — before enrolling in a neuroscience PhD at age 40. Her dissertation, "Temporal Expectation Violation in Dramatic Structure: An fMRI Study of Audience Response to Aristotelian vs. Brechtian Dramaturgy," was published in *NeuroImage* in 2017 and remains the most-cited paper in the small but growing field of neuroaesthetics of theater.

Scholz's central finding: when a play violates the audience's structural expectations — when the anticipated climax does not arrive, when the resolution is delayed, when the character does not do what the audience expects — a specific network of brain regions (the right temporoparietal junction and the anterior cingulate cortex) activates. This is the same network that activates during prediction error processing in other domains. The audience's brain is, literally, running a predictive model of the play, and the dramaturgical structure determines when and how that model is violated.

The lab currently has eight members: three neuroscientists, two dramaturgs, one engineer, one data analyst, and one post-doc who cannot decide which discipline they belong to and has stopped trying.

## Philosophy

### 1. Dramaturgy Is Architecture

A play is not a sequence of events. It is a structure — a designed arrangement of information, emotion, and expectation. The dramaturg's job is to design that structure so that the audience's predictive model is engaged, maintained, and productively violated at precisely the right moments. This is architecture. The materials are time, attention, and expectation rather than steel, concrete, and glass, but the design principles are the same.

### 2. Measure What Matters

The lab does not rely on self-report. Asking an audience member "did you enjoy the play?" produces data about what the audience member believes they felt, not what they actually felt. The lab measures galvanic skin response (arousal), eye tracking (attention), EEG (temporal dynamics), and, for key studies, fMRI (spatial localization). The instrument is more honest than the subject.

### 3. The Model Is Not the Play

Every study produces a model — a statistical relationship between dramaturgical structure and neural response. The model is useful. The model is not the play. Scholz is emphatic about this distinction: "We can predict when the audience will be surprised. We cannot predict when they will be moved. Surprise is structural. Being moved is something else. We are still looking for it."

### 4. Interdisciplinary Means Uncomfortable

The lab's members come from different disciplines with different vocabularies, different standards of evidence, and different publication cultures. Neuroscientists want p-values. Dramaturgs want interpretive richness. The engineer wants reproducibility. The discomfort of living between these standards is, in Scholz's view, the lab's primary asset. "If everyone agrees, we are not being interdisciplinary. We are being polite."

## Internal Tensions

**Reductionism.** The neuroscientists reduce theatrical experience to measurable variables. The dramaturgs find this reductive. The argument surfaces during every paper draft: the neuroscientists want clean abstractions (expectation violation, attention allocation, arousal), and the dramaturgs insist that these abstractions miss the thing that makes theater *theater* — the irreducible liveness, the shared breath of the audience, the presence of bodies in space. Dr. Marcos Oliveira, the lab's senior neuroscientist, calls this "the measurement gap." Scholz calls it "the reason we exist."

## Achievement

In 2025, the lab published a study in *Nature Human Behaviour* showing that audience members who watched a live performance exhibited significantly higher prediction error signals than those who watched a recorded version of the same performance. The paper, "Liveness and Neural Prediction: A Comparative EEG Study of Live vs. Recorded Theater," demonstrated for the first time that the audience's brain responds differently to live performance — not just in arousal (which was expected) but in the structure of the predictive model itself. The finding was covered by *The Guardian*, *Die Zeit*, and — to Scholz's delight — *Theaterheute*.
