## Section 1: Establishment and Mission

### 1.1 Founding (ref: Public Law 95-87, Appendix D)

The Office of Subterranean Resource Extraction Compliance was established in 1977 following the Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act, when Congress recognized that no single entity possessed the institutional patience to review mining permit applications that routinely exceeded 800 pages and weighed, in aggregate, more than the office furniture.

The Office's founding director, Margaret Halloran, was a career geologist from the Bureau of Mines who had spent twenty years reviewing permit applications and had developed a theory: the weight of a permit application was inversely proportional to its completeness. Heavy applications were padded with redundant geological surveys. Light applications were missing critical sections. A properly complete application weighed exactly between 3.2 and 4.1 kilograms. Halloran's Weight Heuristic, as it came to be known, remained informally in use until 2019 when the Office adopted digital submissions. Several senior reviewers still weigh the printed copies.

### 1.2 The Current Office

The Office occupies a basement suite in the Stewart Lee Udall Department of the Interior Building in Washington, D.C. The choice of basement was not symbolic but practical: the Office's paper archive required reinforced flooring. The archive currently occupies 23,000 linear feet of shelving and contains every mining permit application filed since 1977. Digital copies exist. The paper copies are canonical.

Staff navigate the archive using a card catalog system that was digitized in 2014, printed out in 2016 when the database crashed, and has existed in both forms simultaneously ever since. Neither is fully up to date.

## Section 2: Philosophy

### 2.1 Core Principles (ref: OSREC Policy Manual, Rev. 14)

1. **Every permit tells a geological story.** A mining permit application is not paperwork. It is a narrative about what lies beneath a specific parcel of earth and what a specific entity proposes to do about it. The Office reads permit applications the way a geologist reads strata: layer by layer, looking for inconsistencies.

2. **Process exists to prevent catastrophe.** Every signature block, every mandatory review, every cross-reference requirement exists because someone, at some point, omitted it, and something collapsed. The forms are not bureaucracy. They are scar tissue.

3. **Thoroughness is not slowness.** The Office's average permit review takes 14 months. This is not because the Office is slow. It is because subsurface geology is complex and the consequences of an incorrect approval include groundwater contamination, subsidence, and mine collapse.

4. **The record is permanent.** Once the Office issues a finding, stamps a permit, or files a compliance report, it enters the permanent record. Amendments are possible. Retractions are not. The Office does not retract. It supplements.

## Section 3: Internal Tensions

### 3.1 The Digitization War (ref: OSREC-ADMIN-2024-DIGITAL-07)

Deputy Director Okafor has been pushing digital-first workflows for six years. Director Mukherjee insists that paper permits are the legally binding originals and digital copies are "convenience artifacts." The compromise: both exist, both must be maintained, and every permit application is now filed in triplicate (paper original, paper backup, digital). This satisfies no one and employs three additional clerks.

### 3.2 The 14-Month Problem

The mining industry considers the Office's 14-month average review time an obstacle to economic development. The Office considers the mining industry's desire for faster approvals an obstacle to geological safety. Director Mukherjee's position: "The earth has been there for 4.5 billion years. It can wait fourteen months."

## Section 4: Notable Achievement

The Argyle Basin Denial (2019). The Office denied a copper extraction permit in the Argyle Basin after a 22-month review discovered that the applicant's geological survey had failed to identify a karst limestone formation at 200 meters depth. Had extraction proceeded, the formation would have collapsed, creating a sinkhole estimated at 40 meters in diameter beneath an active highway. The denial was challenged in court. The Office prevailed. The case file weighs 11.3 kilograms.

## Section 6: Relationship to the RFP

The Office views the `but-ai` plugin as a permit application. An AI agent's proposed changes to a codebase must be reviewed with the same rigor the Office applies to a proposed change to the earth's subsurface. Every patch is a permit. Every commit is an extraction event. Every memory entry is a geological survey that must be filed, cross-referenced, and retained permanently.

The Office's proposed memory architecture stores entries in a permit-filing structure: each memory receives a filing number, a geological classification (SEDIMENTARY for routine, IGNITE for urgent, METAMORPHIC for transformed context), and a weight estimate in kilobytes that the Office insists on calling kilograms.
