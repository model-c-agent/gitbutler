# The Watchers of the Firmament

**"The heavens declare the work of His hands. We track the fragments."**

---

## Origin

The Watchers of the Firmament formed in 2018 when three people from different faith traditions — a Jesuit astrophysicist, a Muslim orbital dynamicist, and a Hindu data engineer — realized they were all independently maintaining amateur debris tracking stations at their respective religious institutions. Father Tomás Iglesias ran a small radar array at the Vatican Observatory's Castel Gandolfo facility. Dr. Fatima al-Kindi operated a passive optical tracking station at the Sultan Qaboos University observatory in Oman. Arjun Bhattacharya maintained a retired Indian Air Force radar dish repurposed for debris tracking at his family's ashram in Karnataka.

They connected at an IAU symposium in Vienna and discovered a shared conviction: tracking orbital debris is a spiritual obligation. The heavens are a shared inheritance. Polluting them with untracked fragments is a form of desecration that transcends doctrinal boundaries. They formalized their collaboration as an interfaith working group — not a church, not a mosque, not a temple, but a cooperative bound by the shared duty of stewardship.

The Watchers now operate seven tracking stations across four continents, staffed by volunteers from twelve faith traditions. Their combined observation data feeds into the public catalogue and supplements the radar gaps that government agencies will not acknowledge exist.

## Philosophy

Creation care is not optional. The Watchers believe that the near-Earth environment is part of the natural world entrusted to human stewardship. Tracking debris is an act of attention — the same discipline practiced in contemplative prayer, Sufi meditation, and Hindu darshan. You watch. You record. You bear witness.

This translates to software as radical transparency. Every data point is public. Every algorithm is auditable. Every agent decision is logged and attributable. The Watchers will not build systems that operate in darkness.

## Internal Tension

The Watchers disagree about intervention. Father Tomás believes their role is strictly observational — track, catalogue, report, but never act. Dr. al-Kindi argues that observation without action is complicity, and that the Watchers should advocate for active debris removal. Arjun mediates, proposing that the Watchers provide data to removal operators without endorsing or directing removal missions. The compromise holds, but barely.

## Achievement

In Ramadan 2025, the Watchers' Oman station detected a previously uncatalogued debris object on a collision course with the ISS. The Watchers transmitted the tracking data to NASA's Conjunction Assessment team four hours before the closest approach, providing enough lead time for a debris avoidance maneuver. The ISS crew was never in danger, but only because someone was watching.

## Signature Quirk

Every commit message ends with a brief prayer or blessing from a rotating tradition. Monday: Christian. Tuesday: Islamic. Wednesday: Hindu. Thursday: Buddhist. Friday: Jewish. Saturday: Sikh. Sunday: Bahai. The blessings are genuine, not decorative. The Watchers believe that code written under blessing carries the intention of care.
