# Federal Music Standardization Office

**Motto:** *"Compliance is harmony."*

---

## Mandate

The Federal Music Standardization Office (FMSO) is a regulatory agency established by executive order in 2018, housed within the Department of Commerce, Bureau of Standards. Its mission is to develop, publish, and enforce technical standards for music production processes, ensuring interoperability, safety, and quality across the domestic music industry.

FMSO employs 340 staff across three divisions: Standards Development, Compliance Auditing, and Technical Reference. The agency has published 127 standards since its founding, covering topics from reverb decay time measurement methodology (FMSO-STD-0041) to minimum bit depth requirements for archival masters (FMSO-STD-0098). Approximately 14% of these standards are actively enforced. The remainder exist in a liminal state the agency describes as "published for voluntary adoption" and the industry describes as "ignored."

The agency's most controversial standard, FMSO-STD-0023 ("Standard Specification for Snare Drum Reverb Decay Time in Commercial Recordings"), specifies that snare reverb decay shall not exceed 1.2 seconds in recordings distributed for commercial sale. The standard was developed over 18 months by a committee of 23 experts, survives four legal challenges per year, and is universally flouted. FMSO considers this a compliance problem. The industry considers it proof that bureaucracies should not regulate art.

## Why This RFP

The Office's Technology Modernization Task Force identified GitButler's `but-ai` plugin as relevant to FMSO-STD-0112 ("Requirements for Automated Decision Systems in Creative Workflows"). The standard, currently in draft, specifies that any AI system producing artifacts in a regulated creative workflow must maintain an auditable decision log, operate within declared resource budgets, and produce outputs traceable to specific model versions and configurations.

The `but-ai` plugin satisfies several requirements of FMSO-STD-0112. The Task Force recommended participation in the RFP process to (a) ensure the plugin's design is compatible with the forthcoming standard, (b) contribute the agency's expertise in specification development, and (c) justify the Technology Modernization Task Force's continued existence in the next budget cycle.

The Technology Modernization Task Force was created in 2025 to explore "emerging technologies relevant to FMSO's regulatory mission." It has a staff of four, a budget that requires annual justification, and a mandate so broad it can be applied to virtually anything. Anya Petrova, the Task Force lead, is a former NIST engineer who views this breadth as a feature.

## Philosophy

1. **Standards exist to prevent harm.** An unregulated system is a system where the powerful exploit the powerless.
2. **Process is not bureaucracy.** Process is documented decision-making. Bureaucracy is process without purpose. The distinction matters.
3. **Compliance is measurable.** If you cannot measure compliance, the standard is poorly written, not the implementation.

## Internal Tension

The Task Force operates in permanent tension with the Standards Development division. Dr. Munn believes the Office should observe and regulate, not participate and build. He views the RFP response as mission creep -- the Office exists to set standards, not to implement them. Anya argues that standards written without implementation experience are standards that nobody can implement. The Deputy Director has sided with Anya for this proposal but has not committed to future participation.

## Notable Achievement

FMSO-STD-0098 ("Minimum Requirements for Archival Audio Masters") was adopted by the Library of Congress in 2024 as a reference standard for digital preservation. This was the first time an FMSO standard was adopted by an external institution, and it validated the Office's claim that its standards have value beyond the music industry. Dr. Munn celebrated by updating the standard's status from "published" to "published and externally adopted" in the registry. There was cake.
