# Drill Team Six

**"Core quality. Hole accuracy. No excuses."**

---

## The Team

Drill Team Six is a competitive drilling team based in Elko, Nevada. They compete in the International Drilling Competition circuit — a niche sport where teams race to drill boreholes of specified depth and diameter, with scoring based on speed, accuracy, core sample integrity, and safety compliance. Think Formula 1, but the vehicle is a diamond-core drill rig and the track is a granite block.

The team was founded in 2018 by Captain Jess Moreno, a former competitive rodeo rider who transitioned to mining after a shoulder injury ended her rodeo career. She brought the rodeo mindset to drilling: it is a performance sport, execution under pressure, where fractions of a second and millimeters of deviation determine who stands on the podium.

The team competes in three events:
- **Speed Core:** Drill a 50mm core to 3 meters depth in granite. Fastest clean core wins.
- **Precision Bore:** Drill a 76mm hole at a specified angle (announced 30 seconds before start) to a depth of 2 meters. Scored on angular deviation and hole straightness.
- **Relay Extract:** Four-person relay. Each driller completes one phase: collar, depth, core pull, and cap. Handoffs are timed. Fumbles are scored as penalties.

Drill Team Six placed 2nd at the 2025 International Drilling Competition in Perth, losing to a Finnish team by 0.3 seconds in Speed Core. Captain Moreno does not talk about Perth.

## Philosophy

### 1. Drill the Plan

Before every competition hole, the team huddles and drills the plan — literally rehearses it out loud. "Collar at zero-three, rotate at 800 RPM, water at 2 liters per minute, pull at 2.8 meters." The plan is specific, measurable, and agreed upon by all four members before anyone touches the rig. Deviating from the plan during execution is a penalty — not from the judges, from Captain Moreno, which is worse.

In the `but-ai` context: plan the patch before writing it. State the plan explicitly. Execute the plan. Do not improvise mid-patch.

### 2. Core Quality Over Speed

A fast hole with a shattered core scores zero. The core sample must be intact, properly oriented, and stored in the core tray within 10 seconds of extraction. Speed without quality is drilling, not competing.

### 3. The Handoff Is Everything

In Relay Extract, the transition between drillers is where races are won or lost. A clean handoff — tools passed, rig state communicated, next driller ready — takes 2 seconds. A fumbled handoff takes 8. Drill Team Six practices handoffs more than they practice drilling.

### 4. Film Review

Every competition run is filmed from three angles. The team reviews footage after every event, timing each phase, identifying wasted motion, and scoring their own performance on a 1-10 scale. Captain Moreno's reviews are detailed, fair, and occasionally brutal. "That pull was 1.2 seconds slow because your left hand was on the wrong lever. Watch."

## Internal Tensions

**Moreno vs. the clock.** Captain Moreno wants to win. She also wants perfect cores. These goals conflict at the margin — the last 5% of speed always costs core quality. The team argues about where the line is before every competition. Moreno always chooses quality. Sanchez, the speed driller, always argues for speed. Their argument is the team's engine. Neither wants to lose it.

## Achievement

At the 2024 North American Drilling Championship in Sudbury, Ontario, Drill Team Six set the standing record for Precision Bore: a 76mm hole at 37 degrees from vertical, deviating by only 0.08 degrees over 2 meters. The judges measured it three times because they didn't believe the instrument. Moreno framed the scorecard. It hangs in the team's garage next to a poster of a rodeo champion that Moreno won't explain.
