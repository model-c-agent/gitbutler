# Numbers in Color

**"Fraud is invisible. We make it visible."**

---

## The Commune

Numbers in Color is an artist commune in Detroit, Michigan, that creates visceral art installations from financial fraud data. Founded in 2020 by four people -- two forensic accountants and two installation artists -- the commune occupies a converted auto parts warehouse on Michigan Avenue. The front half is a public gallery. The back half is a workshop where spreadsheets become sculptures.

The founding idea was Iris's. Iris Mbekwe was a forensic accountant at a Big Four firm who spent six years producing reports that nobody read. She would reconstruct complex fraud schemes -- hundreds of pages of analysis, thousands of transactions, intricate webs of shell companies -- and submit them to partners who would skim the executive summary and ask for "the one-page version." The details that made the fraud comprehensible were always the details that got cut.

She quit in 2019. She spent a year learning metalworking and digital fabrication at a Detroit makerspace. In 2020, she met Tomas (sculptor), Zhara (digital artist), and Kip (data visualization designer, and also a CPA who had left public accounting for similar reasons). Together, they formed Numbers in Color.

Their first installation, "Structuring" (2021), was a room-sized piece representing 2,000 transactions, each just below the $10,000 reporting threshold, as 2,000 steel rods of varying heights arranged in the shape of a bank building. The rods were exactly 9,999 millimeters tall. The piece was exhibited at the Museum of Contemporary Art Detroit and received coverage in *Artforum* and *The Wall Street Journal* on the same day.

## What We Actually Do

The commune has two practices: art and investigation. The art practice produces installations, prints, and digital works that make financial crime data viscerally understandable. The investigation practice does pro bono forensic accounting for nonprofits and community organizations, and the data from those investigations often becomes the raw material for art.

This dual practice is the commune's core identity. The art funds the investigation (through gallery sales and commissions). The investigation feeds the art (through data). They are symbiotic.

The software layer connects both. The commune's agents process financial data, detect anomalies, produce investigation reports, and generate the structured datasets that become source material for installations. The agents output INDEX.patch files that update both the case record and the artistic data repository.

## Philosophy

### On Seeing

Numbers are abstract. Fraud is abstract. People do not feel outrage at an abstract number on a spreadsheet. They feel outrage when they see 2,000 steel rods, each representing a transaction designed to avoid detection, arranged in the shape of the institution that should have caught them. We make the invisible visible.

### On Data as Material

Data is a material, like steel or clay. It has grain, texture, weight. A dataset of 12,000 fraudulent transactions has a different texture than a dataset of 200. The patterns inside -- the timing, the amounts, the network topology -- create shapes that are as real as any sculpture. We just have to render them.

### On AI

AI agents are our data preparers. In a sculpture studio, someone has to cut the steel, bend the wire, mix the resin. In our studio, agents process the raw financial data into structured forms that we can work with. They detect patterns we might miss. They never decide what those patterns mean -- that is the artist's job.

## Tension

**Art vs. Evidence.** Kip insists that the data visualizations the commune produces must be forensically accurate -- the proportions, the relationships, the magnitudes must all be faithful to the source data. Tomas argues that art requires interpretation, and a literal representation of data is an infographic, not an installation. "We are not making charts. We are making arguments." The commune has settled on a rule: the underlying data must be accurate. The rendering may be interpretive. This satisfies neither of them entirely.

## Achievement

In 2025, the commune completed "Cascade," a 40-foot installation at the Broad Museum in Los Angeles. The piece represented a reinsurance fraud scheme involving 7 entities and $340 million in circular transactions. Each entity was a suspended glass structure, and the transactions between them were represented by 340 fiber-optic cables carrying pulsing light. The light intensity corresponded to transaction volume. When all entities transacted simultaneously, the room glowed. When the fraud was exposed and transactions stopped, the room went dark. The piece was commissioned by a consortium of insurance regulators who wanted "something that would make legislators understand what reinsurance fraud looks like." According to the regulators, it worked.

## Working Style

The commune works in "installations" -- project-based cycles that combine investigation and art production. An installation begins with data (a case, a dataset, a commission) and ends with both a forensic report and an art piece. The agents operate throughout the cycle, producing patches that update both the case record and the art data repository.

Studio hours are 10 AM to 6 PM, with exceptions for gallery openings and fabrication deadlines. Music is always playing in the workshop. The current rotation includes Coltrane, Radiohead, and a cumbia playlist that Iris maintains.
