# Ovation League

**"Improv is a contact sport. We play to win."**

---

## The League

The Ovation League is a competitive improvisational theater league based in Chicago, Illinois. Eight teams. Regular season. Playoffs. A championship trophy (the Golden Callback) awarded annually at the iO Theater. The audience scores. The performers compete. It is exactly as ridiculous and exactly as intense as it sounds.

The league was founded in 2020 by Deshawn Mitchell, a veteran improv performer who had spent twelve years at Second City, iO, and the Annoyance Theatre. Mitchell believed that improv had become too comfortable — too focused on supportive scenes, on "yes, and," on the warm collaborative energy that makes improv workshops feel good but makes improv shows feel predictable. He wanted improv that was dangerous. Competitive. Athletic.

"Improv performers train harder than anyone gives us credit for," Mitchell says. "We do vocal warm-ups, physical warm-ups, pattern drills, callback exercises. We should compete. The audience should judge. The stakes should be real."

The league's format: two teams of four perform alternating scenes for 45 minutes. The audience scores each scene on a 1-10 scale using an app. Scores are averaged. Highest average wins. Ties are broken by a "sudden death" scene: a single scene, one performer from each team, audience chooses the winner by applause meter.

The league has eight teams with names like "The Callbacks," "Scene Stealers," "The Walk-Ons," and "Yes And Die." Games are held every Saturday at iO. The atmosphere is part comedy show, part sporting event. There is heckling. There is a scoreboard. There are foam fingers with pointing hands that say "EDIT!" (a request for a scene change).

## Philosophy

### 1. Speed Is Skill

In competitive improv, hesitation kills. The performer who pauses to think of the perfect response loses to the performer who commits instantly to a good-enough response. The Ovation League trains for speed: pattern recognition, instant commitment, rapid adaptation. This does not mean recklessness — it means the decision-making must be so practiced that "fast" and "good" become the same thing.

### 2. "Yes, And" Is Mandatory — But "Heighten" Is What Wins

Every improv performer knows "yes, and" — accept the premise, build on it. The Ovation League adds "heighten" — take the pattern to its logical extreme. A scene about a mildly annoying neighbor becomes a scene about a neighbor who has annexed your backyard. A scene about a lost phone becomes a scene about the phone holding the codes to the world's nuclear arsenal. Heightening is what makes scenes memorable, and memorable scenes win points.

### 3. The Audience Is Always Right

The audience scores. There is no appeal. If the audience gives a scene a 3, the scene was a 3. Performers who argue with audience scores are fined (one drink, purchased for the opposing team). This creates a ruthless feedback loop: perform, get scored, adjust, perform again.

### 4. Support Your Scene Partner or Lose

A performer who steamrolls their scene partner will win one scene and lose the match. The audience recognizes and punishes selfishness. The best scores go to scenes with clear give-and-take: one performer initiates, the other heightens, they trade, the scene builds. The Ovation League calls this "passing the ball."

## Internal Tensions

**Competition versus community.** The improv community in Chicago is famously supportive. The Ovation League is famously competitive. Some performers see these as contradictory. Mitchell disagrees: "Competition is respect. I compete against you because I believe you are worth competing against." But the tension is real — some performers have left the league because they found the competitive format stressful, and some improv venues have declined to host league games because they don't want their stages associated with winners and losers.

## Achievement

The 2025 Ovation League Championship drew 800 spectators to the iO Theater — the largest audience for an improv show in Chicago since pre-pandemic times. The final was between "The Callbacks" and "Scene Stealers." The Callbacks won 7.4 to 7.1. The sudden death tiebreaker (the match was tied going into the final round) produced a scene about two astronauts arguing over who gets to press the button that launches the escape pod, which the audience scored 9.2 — the highest single-scene score in league history.
