# Rasmussen Mining Works

*Est. 1887 — Hammerfest, Norway*

---

## Five Generations

The Rasmussens have been mining copper from the same mountain in northern Norway since 1887, when Anders Rasmussen — a blacksmith who noticed green staining on the rocks near his forge — dug an exploratory tunnel with a pick and a lantern. He found chalcopyrite at twelve meters. His son Karl expanded the tunnel into a proper adit. Karl's daughter Ingrid mechanized the operation in 1953. Ingrid's son Erik computerized it in 1989. Erik's daughter Liv runs it now.

The mine is called Grunnfjellet — "the bedrock" in Norwegian. It is not a large operation. It produces approximately 800 tonnes of copper concentrate per year, which places it firmly in the "boutique" category by global mining standards. The Rasmussens do not compete with Rio Tinto. They do not want to. They mine what the mountain gives, at the rate the mountain permits, and they have done so for 139 years.

The family office is in the original Rasmussen farmhouse, 200 meters from the mine entrance. The farmhouse has been continuously occupied by Rasmussens since 1872. Liv's office is in the room where Anders was born. The desk is new. The view of the mountain is not.

## Philosophy

### 1. The Mountain Was Here First

The Rasmussens do not own Grunnfjellet. They hold a concession — a right to extract, granted by the Norwegian state, renewed every 25 years. The renewal has never been denied, but Liv prepares for it as if it might be, every time. "The mountain was here before us. It will be here after us. We are guests."

### 2. Five-Generation Thinking

Every decision at Rasmussen Mining Works is evaluated against a five-generation horizon. Will this decision still make sense when Liv's grandchildren run the mine? This eliminates short-term optimization. It also eliminates most technology fads. The Rasmussens adopted electric vehicles for underground haulage in 2021 — not because EVs were trendy, but because Liv calculated that diesel fuel costs over the next 125 years made electrification obviously correct.

### 3. One Mine, Done Well

The Rasmussens have been offered partnerships, acquisitions, and expansion opportunities for decades. They decline them all. "We know this mountain," Liv says. "We do not know other mountains. Knowing a mountain takes a century."

## Internal Tensions

**Liv and her father Erik disagree about automation.** Erik, now 72 and nominally retired, visits the mine weekly and objects to Liv's plans to introduce autonomous drilling rigs. "The mine runs on people who know the rock," he says. "A machine does not know the rock." Liv counters that the machine can learn from 139 years of drilling data — data that Erik himself digitized. They argue about this over Thursday dinner at the farmhouse, which is also the weekly operations meeting. Erik always brings wine. Liv always brings spreadsheets. Neither convinces the other. The mine continues to operate with a mix of manual and semi-automated equipment that satisfies neither fully.

## Achievement

In 2023, the Norwegian Geological Survey published a study of Grunnfjellet's copper deposit and described the Rasmussen family's 139-year geological dataset as "the most complete continuous record of a single copper deposit in Scandinavia." The dataset includes hand-drawn geological maps from 1892, typed assay reports from the 1950s, and digital sensor data from the 2020s. Liv donated a copy to the University of Troms. She kept the originals in the farmhouse safe.
