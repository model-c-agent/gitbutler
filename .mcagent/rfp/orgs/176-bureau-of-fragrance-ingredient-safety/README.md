# Bureau of Fragrance Ingredient Safety

**"No molecule ships without our stamp. No stamp ships without our review."**

---

## Statutory Authority

The Bureau of Fragrance Ingredient Safety (BFIS) is a regulatory division within the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA), established under Regulation (EC) No. 1907/2006 (REACH) with specific competence over fragrance and flavour ingredients used in consumer products sold within the European Economic Area. The Bureau operates from ECHA's headquarters in Helsinki, Finland, occupying the third floor of the Annankatu building.

The Bureau's mandate: evaluate the safety of fragrance ingredients, maintain the authorised ingredient registry, and issue or deny marketing authorizations for novel aromatic compounds. Every new fragrance molecule sold in the EEA must pass through BFIS review. The review process takes, on average, 14 months per molecule. There are currently 312 molecules in the review queue.

Director Anneli Virtanen has led the Bureau since its reorganization in 2018. She is a toxicologist by training, a bureaucrat by vocation, and patient by constitutional necessity. She has approved 847 molecules and denied 93 in her tenure. She has never reversed a denial. She has reversed two approvals, both after new toxicological data emerged. Each reversal required four months of procedural work and generated approximately 200 pages of documentation.

## How We Arrived At This RFP

In 2025, the Bureau was directed by the ECHA Management Board to "explore computational tools for accelerating the ingredient review pipeline without compromising scientific rigour." The directive was motivated by industry complaints that the 14-month average review time was delaying product launches and driving fragrance companies to register novel ingredients in jurisdictions with faster approval processes.

Director Virtanen assembled a working group. The working group produced a 64-page report recommending, among other things, the use of AI agents to automate the literature review phase of ingredient evaluation — the phase where a toxicologist reads every published study on a molecule and summarises the findings. This phase alone accounts for 5 of the 14 months.

Virtanen approved a pilot program. The pilot uses AI agents to produce structured literature summaries that a human toxicologist then reviews and approves. The agents need version control because the literature review is iterative — new studies are published during the review period, and the summary must be updated without losing the work already completed.

A member of the IT department found the `but-ai` RFP and forwarded it to Virtanen with a one-line email: "This might be relevant." Virtanen read the RFP, re-read it, and forwarded it to the working group with the instruction: "Respond. Follow the format. Do not promise anything we cannot deliver."

## Tension

**The Speed-Rigour Tradeoff.** The Management Board wants faster reviews. Dr. Alexandrou wants thorough reviews. His position: "A molecule approved on incomplete evidence and later found to be a sensitizer causes more harm than a molecule delayed by six months." Virtanen agrees with him scientifically and with the Management Board politically. Her compromise: AI agents handle literature review (speed), but human toxicologists make all safety determinations (rigour). The agents prepare the evidence; humans weigh it.

## Achievement

In 2023, the Bureau identified a potential genotoxicity concern with methyl dihydrojasmonate (a widely used floral ingredient) based on a re-evaluation triggered by Dr. Alexandrou's review of three new in-vitro studies. The Bureau issued a restriction proposal that, after 18 months of public consultation and committee review, resulted in a reduced maximum concentration limit across all product categories. The restriction affected over 4,000 products on the European market. Dr. Alexandrou considers it the most important safety action of his career. The fragrance industry considers it overreach. Virtanen considers it procedure.
