# Nosebleed Studio

**"We bottle what is disappearing."**

---

## What We Are

Nosebleed Studio is an artist collective in Reykjavik, Iceland, that captures the smells of ecosystems under threat of extinction. Peat bogs drying from climate change. Old-growth forests being logged. Coral reefs bleaching. The Studio sends field teams to these places, captures their volatile organic compounds using headspace analysis (trapping the gases above a sample in a sealed container), analyzes the chemical composition via GC-MS, and then recreates the smell as a wearable perfume.

The perfumes are not commercial products. They are artworks. Each one is an edition of 100 bottles, sold at gallery prices, with proceeds funding the next field expedition. The Studio has produced eleven editions since 2020. The most acclaimed: "Bleach" (the smell of a dying coral reef in the Maldives), "Char" (the smell of a controlled burn in an Australian eucalyptus forest), and "Peat" (the smell of a Scottish bog that will be dry within twenty years).

The Studio was founded by Sigrid Jonsdottir, a perfumer who trained at ISIPCA and worked at Symrise before a residency at the Icelandic Academy of the Arts changed her direction. During the residency, she climbed Eyjafjallajokull and was overwhelmed by the smell of sulfur, moss, and glacial melt — a smell that was changing year by year as the glacier retreated. She decided to capture it. The resulting perfume, "Retreating" (2020), became the Studio's founding work.

## Philosophy

A smell is a chemical fingerprint of a moment in an ecosystem's life. When the ecosystem dies, the smell dies with it. Photography preserves how a place looks. Sound recording preserves how it sounds. Nothing preserves how it smells. Until now.

The Studio does not believe in preservation for its own sake. Preservation without context is taxidermy. Each perfume is accompanied by a monograph — written by the field team — documenting the ecosystem, the threats, the organisms that produce the volatile compounds, and the human communities that live alongside them. The perfume is an entry point. The monograph is the work.

## Why This RFP

In 2025, the Studio began using AI agents to accelerate the analytical phase of their workflow. Field collection produces GC-MS chromatograms — peaks on a graph, each representing a volatile compound. Identifying those compounds and matching them to natural sources is the bottleneck. A single chromatogram from a forest headspace sample might contain 200 peaks, each requiring identification.

Multiple agents process different regions of the chromatogram simultaneously. Their identifications are structured as patches to a shared compound inventory. Conflicts arise when two agents identify the same peak differently (a common occurrence with co-eluting compounds — molecules that have nearly identical retention times on the chromatography column).

Sigrid found GitButler through an art-tech forum. The virtual branch model was the first VCS she encountered that treated conflicting identifications not as errors to be resolved but as concurrent hypotheses to be held until evidence decides.

## Tension

**The Fidelity Debate.** Takeshi insists that the recreated perfume must be chemically faithful to the field sample — every identified compound present at the correct concentration. Sigrid argues that fidelity to the chromatogram is not fidelity to the experience. A bog does not smell like a list of terpenes. It smells like cold and wet and time. She adjusts the formulas for emotional accuracy, adding compounds that were not in the sample but evoke the feeling. Takeshi considers this unscientific. Sigrid considers it art.

## Achievement

In 2024, "Peat" was acquired by the Tate Modern for its permanent collection. It was the first olfactory artwork in the Tate's holdings. The acquisition included 10 bottles of the perfume, the GC-MS chromatogram, the field monograph, and a sealed headspace sample from the original bog, stored in a nitrogen-flushed glass ampoule. The curator described it as "a time capsule you can smell." Sigrid described it as "a eulogy." The bog is expected to dry completely by 2038.
