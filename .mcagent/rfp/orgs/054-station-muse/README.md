# Station Muse

**"A beautiful station is a used station. We have the numbers."**

---

## Origin

Station Muse began in 2017 as a guerrilla art project. Lena Zhao, a public artist in Montreal, and Kwame Asante, a transit planner at the STM (Societe de transport de Montreal), were dating and arguing about bus shelters. Kwame said bus shelters are functional objects — weather protection, schedule display, bench. Lena said bus shelters are the most viewed public art installation in any city and treating them as purely functional was a waste of the most democratic gallery space on earth.

They made a bet. Lena would redesign three bus shelters on Kwame's least-used routes. If ridership increased, Kwame would buy dinner for a year. If it did not, Lena would stop complaining about bus shelters.

Lena redesigned the shelters with hand-painted murals, integrated lighting that responded to weather, and acoustic panels that dampened traffic noise while amplifying birdsong from a speaker hidden in the roof. Ridership on the three routes increased 18% over four months. Kwame bought a lot of dinners.

Station Muse incorporated in 2019. They now work with transit agencies in seven cities, combining public art installations with rigorous ridership analytics. Every design decision is backed by data: color palettes tested against dwell time, seating configurations correlated with passenger satisfaction surveys, lighting spectra measured against perceived safety.

## Philosophy

Beauty is infrastructure. A transit system that people want to use is a transit system that works. Station Muse believes that aesthetics and analytics are not in tension — they are the same discipline viewed from different angles. Data tells you where people are. Art brings them there.

They approach AI agents the same way: an agent's output should be correct AND elegant. A patch that works but is ugly — inconsistent naming, awkward structure, gratuitous complexity — is a patch that makes the codebase less inviting. Less inviting means fewer contributors. Fewer contributors means slower maintenance. Aesthetics compound.

## Internal Tension

Lena and Kwame disagree about measurement. Kwame wants to measure everything: dwell time, satisfaction scores, ridership deltas. Lena thinks over-measurement kills the magic — that some of what makes a station beautiful is irreducible to metrics. Kwame counters that unmeasured beauty is indistinguishable from expensive decoration. The tension fuels their best work: designs that are both measured and magical.

## Achievement

Station Muse's redesign of the Parc Avenue metro entrance in Montreal won the 2025 APTA Design Award and increased daily entries by 22%. The design used locally-sourced recycled glass, generative lighting patterns driven by real-time passenger flow data, and a soundscape composed by a local musician. The total cost was 15% less than the standard renovation budget because Station Muse designed the art and the infrastructure together, eliminating the usual "art add-on" premium.

## Signature Quirk

Every commit message includes a color hex code representing the "mood" of the change. Bug fixes are cool blues (#2196F3). New features are warm ambers (#FF8F00). Refactors are neutral greys (#9E9E9E). The team selects the color collectively in their Slack channel. Disagreements about color choice have lasted longer than the actual code changes.
