# Open Stacks Alliance

*Every book belongs to everyone. Every community deserves a library.*

---

## The Alliance

The Open Stacks Alliance is a network of volunteer librarians, archivists, and technologists who build and maintain shadow libraries for communities that have lost access to institutional libraries. When a public library closes — due to budget cuts, natural disaster, or political decision — the Alliance arrives with boxes of donated books, a laptop, a portable WiFi hotspot, and a catalog system that runs on a Raspberry Pi.

The Alliance was founded in 2019 by Miriam Okonkwo, a public librarian in Detroit who watched three branch libraries close in a single year. The closures hit the neighborhoods that needed libraries most — communities where the library was the only place with free internet, free air conditioning, and an adult who would help a child with homework. Miriam spent six months trying to reverse the closures through official channels. When that failed, she loaded her car with 400 books from a Friends of the Library sale and drove to a community center in the affected neighborhood. She set up the books on folding tables, connected her laptop to a hotspot, and opened a catalog. The first patron arrived within twenty minutes.

The Alliance now operates 34 shadow libraries across 12 states. They are technically unauthorized — they do not hold library charters, they do not pay licensing fees to publishers, and their collections include digitized materials of ambiguous copyright status. The Alliance considers this civil disobedience. Publishers consider it piracy. The legal question has not been tested in court, mostly because no publisher wants the optics of suing a volunteer library in a neighborhood that lost its public library.

## Philosophy

### 1. Access Is the Right, Not the Service

Libraries are not a service provided by institutions. They are a right exercised by communities. When the institution fails, the community's right does not disappear — it goes unserved. The Alliance serves it.

### 2. The Catalog Is the Library

A collection of books without a catalog is a pile. A catalog without books is a library waiting to be stocked. The Alliance prioritizes the catalog over the collection, because the catalog is the structure that makes knowledge findable. Their catalog system — OpenCat, built on the Raspberry Pi — uses a simplified MARC-like schema that volunteer librarians can learn in an afternoon.

### 3. Everything Open, Everything Shared

The Alliance uses open-source software, open data formats, and open catalogs. Every shadow library's catalog is published to the Alliance's shared index. A patron in Detroit can find a book in a shadow library in Memphis and request an inter-library loan (which, in the Alliance's case, means someone puts the book in a padded envelope and mails it).

### 4. Radical Hospitality

Every shadow library is open to everyone. No ID. No library card (unless requested — some patrons like library cards). No fines. No due dates (the honor system works better than Miriam expected and worse than she hoped). The point is not to control access. The point is to provide it.

## Internal Tensions

**Legality.** Half the Alliance's members believe they should operate fully within the law — accepting only donated physical books, avoiding any digitized materials, and obtaining proper permits for their pop-up locations. The other half believes that legality and justice are not the same thing, and that a community without a library deserves access to knowledge regardless of copyright law. Miriam navigates this carefully: "We follow the law where we can. We follow our conscience where we must. We document everything so that if we are ever tested, our motives are clear."

## Achievement

In 2025, the Alliance's shadow library in Flint, Michigan — operating from a converted laundromat — served 12,000 patrons, making it the highest-circulation library in its zip code. (The nearest public library branch is a 40-minute bus ride away.) The Michigan Library Association, which initially opposed the Alliance's operations, invited Miriam to speak at their annual conference. Her talk, "Libraries Without Walls, Librarians Without Permission," received a standing ovation and no official endorsement.

*The Alliance has no headquarters. Miriam works from her kitchen table in Detroit. Tomás codes from a bus in Mexico City (literally — he commutes two hours each way and codes the entire time). Fatima is in a co-working space in Amman. Kwame is in a community center in Accra. They have met in person once, at the Michigan Library Association conference. They communicate via Matrix, an open-source chat protocol, because the Alliance does not use proprietary communication tools on principle.*
