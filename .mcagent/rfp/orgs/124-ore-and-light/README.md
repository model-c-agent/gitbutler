# Ore & Light

**"What the earth discards, we illuminate."**

---

## Origin

Ore & Light is an artist commune based in an abandoned copper mine in the mountains outside Bisbee, Arizona. The mine closed in 1985. The commune moved in — legally, after purchasing the surface rights for $1 — in 2014.

The founder, Callista Vega, is a sculptor who works exclusively with mine tailings: the crusite, copper slag, and acid-mine drainage residue that are the toxic legacy of a century of copper extraction. Her sculptures are simultaneously art objects and remediation projects. The copper slag she fires in her kiln binds heavy metals into inert glass. The acid-mine drainage she filters through limestone installations neutralizes pH while creating mineral formations that she exhibits as "geological self-portraits." Every piece she sells funds additional remediation work on the mine site.

Callista's partner, Dean Oduya, is a structural engineer who designed load-bearing sculptures from mine waste — art installations that also serve as retaining walls, drainage channels, and erosion barriers. Together, they attracted a community of artists, engineers, and environmental scientists who share the conviction that mining's waste is not garbage — it is raw material for something better.

The commune's software practice began when Hazel Park, a data visualization artist and former Google engineer, joined in 2020 and started building tools to track remediation progress: pH levels in drainage, heavy metal concentrations in soil, vegetation regrowth rates. The data was beautiful — literally — and Hazel started generating artistic visualizations that showed remediation progress as color fields and topographic maps. The visualizations were exhibited alongside the physical sculptures.

When AI agents became practical, Hazel saw an opportunity: agents could automate the remediation monitoring pipeline, generate visualization updates, and coordinate between the environmental sensors, the data processing systems, and the gallery display software. The commune's `but-ai` proposal extends this pipeline-coordination experience to general-purpose code generation.

## Philosophy

**Waste is raw material. Failure is data.** Everything the mine discarded — the slag, the acid water, the spent rock — has value if you know how to transform it. Every failed agent run, every rejected patch, every expired memory has value if you know how to learn from it. The commune does not delete failed artifacts. They archive them, analyze them, and sometimes exhibit them.

## The Tension

Callista and Dean disagree about the relationship between beauty and function. Callista believes the aesthetic quality of output matters — a well-structured patch that reads cleanly is better than an equivalent patch that is functionally correct but ugly. Dean, the structural engineer, argues that function is the only metric: "A retaining wall that holds but is ugly is better than a beautiful wall that collapses." Hazel, caught in the middle, builds systems that produce both: functional code with clean formatting, because she believes the argument is a false dichotomy.

## Notable Achievement

In 2025, the commune's remediation monitoring system detected a spike in arsenic concentrations downstream of the mine site — a level that was within regulatory limits but trending upward. The AI agent managing the monitoring pipeline automatically generated a report, flagged the trend, and opened a PR in the environmental data repository with a proposed sampling plan. The commune implemented the plan, identified a previously unknown tailings deposit as the source, and remediated it before the arsenic level exceeded limits. The agent's early detection saved an estimated $200,000 in emergency remediation costs and prevented contamination of a local aquifer.

## Working Style

The commune works in a converted assay office — the room where miners once tested ore samples. The walls are lined with Callista's smaller sculptures. Hazel's monitors display live remediation data rendered as abstract color fields. Dean's structural drawings are pinned to a corkboard beside the window.

Work happens in "studio hours" — 10 AM to 6 PM, with a communal lunch break where the team eats together and discusses both art and code. No one works after 6 PM. The evening is for making art, cooking, or sitting on the mine's observation deck watching the Arizona sunset paint the tailings piles in colors that Callista says are "the earth's own gallery."
