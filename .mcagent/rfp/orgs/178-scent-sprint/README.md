# Scent Sprint

**"Nose Olympics. No goggles required."**

---

## What We Do

Scent Sprint is a competitive olfactory athletics organization — yes, that is a real thing — based in Lyon, France. We organize and promote competitive fragrance identification: athletes are presented with blind samples of aromatic compounds and must identify them by smell alone. Fastest correct identification wins. The sport has existed informally since at least the 1970s (perfumery students in Grasse would challenge each other at bars), but Scent Sprint formalized it in 2021 with structured rules, weight classes based on experience level, and an annual championship.

Founded by Coach Didier Faure, a retired perfumer who spent 35 years at IFF and grew bored in retirement. He missed the daily act of smelling and identifying. He did not miss the corporate politics. He started hosting informal identification challenges at a café in Lyon, timing participants with a stopwatch. Word spread. By 2021, he had sixty regular participants and a waiting list.

The sport works like this: competitors sit at a station. An automated olfactometer delivers a precisely metered dose of a single aroma chemical. The competitor says the name of the compound. A judge verifies. Time is recorded. Best-of-ten rounds. Fastest cumulative time wins. The current world record for a single identification is 0.8 seconds (Bergamot oil, identified by a Grasse-trained perfumer named Celine Moreau who retired from competition in 2024 and now coaches the junior team).

We have 240 registered athletes across three experience classes: Apprentice (fewer than 2 years of training), Journeyman (2-10 years), and Master (10+ years or professional perfumers). The annual championship draws 80 competitors and a surprisingly passionate spectator crowd who watch athletes sniff and shout compound names with the intensity of a tennis match.

## Why We Are Here

In 2025, Coach Faure began exploring AI to improve training analytics. The insight: competitive identification is trainable. Athletes who practice with systematic exposure to structurally similar compounds improve faster than athletes who practice randomly. An AI agent that designs personalized training sequences — "today, practice distinguishing linalool from linalyl acetate, then move to geraniol vs. citronellol" — could accelerate skill development.

The agents needed to track athlete progress, design training sequences, and coordinate between coaches. The training plans were structured as sequential patches to an athlete's curriculum. Conflicts arose when two coaches independently updated the same athlete's training plan. GitButler's virtual branches resolved this: each coach works on a virtual branch, and the athlete's training plan merges the best elements of each.

## Tension

**The Natural Talent Debate.** Dr. Diallo believes olfactory identification ability is almost entirely trainable — that with the right curriculum, anyone can reach Journeyman level. Coach Faure disagrees. He has seen natural talent. Celine Moreau could identify compounds on first exposure that some athletes never master after years of training. "You cannot train a nose that was not built for it," he says. Diallo considers this a harmful myth that discourages novice athletes. Their argument shapes the training system: Diallo's training plans assume everyone can improve; Faure's evaluations acknowledge that some ceilings are genetic.

## Achievement

In 2024, Scent Sprint hosted the first International Olfactory Athletics Championship in Lyon. Sixty-three athletes from twelve countries competed across three days. The event was covered by Le Monde (bemused), the perfume trade press (enthusiastic), and a Japanese television crew (who produced a documentary that became unexpectedly popular). The championship established Scent Sprint as the de facto governing body for competitive olfactory athletics. Coach Faure gave the opening speech in French, English, and (poorly) Japanese, and received a standing ovation he later described as "the loudest noise at the quietest sport in the world."
