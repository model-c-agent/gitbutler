# Maison Lefevre

**"Grand-pere coupe. Maman drape. Je code. Meme table."**

---

## Three Generations, One Room

Maison Lefevre operates from a narrow atelier on Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honore in Paris, the same space the family has occupied since 1962. The cutting table is original -- scarred oak, seven meters long, with burn marks from Pierre Lefevre's cigarettes in the 1970s. His granddaughter Chloe now codes at one end of it while her mother Isabelle drapes fabric at the other.

Pierre Lefevre (88, semi-retired) founded the atelier as a bespoke menswear house. His daughter Isabelle (59) expanded it into womenswear and introduced CAD in 1998. Isabelle's daughter Chloe (31) joined in 2020 after a computer science degree at Ecole Polytechnique and three years at a Parisian fashion-tech startup that failed. Three generations. Three skill sets. One table.

The business model is traditional haute couture: made-to-measure garments for private clients, with prices that ensure the family can afford to keep the atelier open and the six full-time seamstresses employed. Revenue has been flat for a decade. Chloe believes AI-assisted pattern generation and version-controlled design workflows can reduce production costs by 20% without sacrificing the handwork that clients pay for.

Pierre believes computers are "une distraction." He is not entirely wrong.

## How We Got Here

Chloe introduced Git to the atelier in 2022 for managing digital patterns. Isabelle adapted quickly -- she had been using Illustrator since 2001 and understood versioning intuitively. Pierre refused to touch a computer but agreed to let Chloe scan his hand-cut patterns into the system "as long as the original paper is preserved."

GitButler's virtual branches solved a real problem: Isabelle and Chloe often work on variants of the same garment simultaneously. Before virtual branches, they used separate files with names like `blazer_isabelle_v3_FINAL_actually_final.svg`. After virtual branches, they work on the same file in parallel branches and merge when ready.

The `but-ai` RFP interested Chloe because she sees AI agents as a third generation of tool: Pierre's hands, Isabelle's CAD, Chloe's algorithms. Each generation augmented the previous one without replacing it.

## Philosophy

The family does not agree on philosophy. Pierre believes craft is transmission -- you learn by watching, not by reading documentation. Isabelle believes craft is adaptation -- the tools change, the standards do not. Chloe believes craft is automation of the automatable, freeing the human for the irreplaceable.

These three philosophies coexist in the same room, at the same table, every day. It works because family arguments have a different cadence than professional disagreements. Nobody storms out. They argue over lunch and resume work after coffee.

## Internal Tension

**The Scanning Incident.** In 2024, Chloe scanned Pierre's last remaining undigitized pattern -- a 1967 smoking jacket template -- without asking. Pierre was furious, not because of the scanning itself, but because Chloe had not asked his permission. "The pattern is mine until I give it," he said. The family now has a formal protocol: Pierre's patterns are scanned only with his explicit verbal consent, witnessed by Isabelle. Chloe finds this excessive. Isabelle finds it respectful. Pierre finds it "the minimum."

## Notable Achievement

The 2025 Bridal Collection was the first Maison Lefevre line produced with AI-assisted grading. Twelve bespoke gowns, each with between 3 and 7 size adjustments, produced in 60% of the usual time. Client satisfaction scores were unchanged -- the handwork was identical, only the preparatory pattern math was automated. Pierre examined every gown and pronounced them "acceptable," which from Pierre is high praise.
