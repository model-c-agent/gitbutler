# The Players of St. Genesius

*Patron saint of actors, comedians, and converts.*

---

## The Company

The Players of St. Genesius are a Catholic theater company based in New Haven, Connecticut, that produces Broadway-quality morality plays. Not the medieval kind — or rather, not only the medieval kind. The Players produce contemporary original works that engage with moral questions through theatrical craft: professional actors, professional designers, professional directors, performed in a 400-seat theater attached to the Dominican priory on Hillhouse Avenue.

The company was founded in 1998 by Father Thomas Brennan, O.P., a Dominican friar with a Yale School of Drama MFA and a conviction that the Church had ceded the arts to secular culture unnecessarily. "Morality plays have a bad reputation," Brennan told the *New York Times* in 2004, "because bad artists made bad morality plays. The solution is not to abandon the form. It is to make good ones."

Brennan recruited actors from the New Haven theater community — several of whom were skeptical of performing for a religious company until they read the scripts. The scripts were sharp, literate, and dramatically rigorous. They were also, unmistakably, morality plays: stories in which characters confront ethical choices with real consequences, in which virtue and vice are made visible through action, in which the audience is invited to examine their own conscience. They were not sermons. They were plays. The distinction matters.

The company has won three Connecticut Critics Circle Awards and been reviewed favorably by *The New York Times*, *American Theatre*, and — improbably — *Variety*, which described their 2023 production *The Accountant's Confession* as "the most thrilling thing I've seen about double-entry bookkeeping."

## Philosophy

### 1. Beauty Is a Moral Argument

The Players believe that theatrical beauty — elegant staging, precise acting, luminous design — is not decoration. It is a moral argument. A morality play performed poorly is moralism. A morality play performed beautifully is art. The audience does not need to be told what is right. They need to see what is beautiful and recognize the moral truth within it.

### 2. The Script Is the Examination of Conscience

Every Players script undergoes what Father Brennan calls the "examination of conscience" — a dramaturgy process modeled on the Ignatian spiritual exercise. The dramaturg reads the script and asks: Where does this character face a genuine choice between good and evil? Is the choice difficult? Is the good genuinely costly? Is the evil genuinely tempting? If the answers are no, the script is not ready.

### 3. Craft Is Devotion

The Players rehearse six days a week for five weeks before opening. This is unusual for a non-commercial theater company. Brennan insists: "Sloppy work is not humble. It is disrespectful — to the audience, to the text, and to God, who gave us the capacity for craft."

## Internal Tensions

**Art versus evangelization.** Some members of the Dominican community believe the Players should be more explicitly evangelical — that the plays should lead audiences toward conversion. Brennan and the company resist. "If the play is good enough, the audience will encounter the truth. That encounter is not our job to manage. Our job is the play." The tension surfaces every time a script is ambiguous about its moral conclusion. The 2024 production *The Wager* ended without resolving whether the protagonist's sacrifice was redemptive or futile. The priory's newsletter called it "challenging." Brennan called it "honest."

## Achievement

*The Accountant's Confession* (2023) transferred to an Off-Broadway run at the Lucille Lortel Theatre, making the Players the first religious theater company to have a production run Off-Broadway since the Living Theatre (which was religious in a very different way). The production ran for eight weeks and was nominated for a Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Play.
