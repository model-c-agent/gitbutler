## Institutional History

The Department of Comprehensive Zoning Review (DCZR) was established in 1987 as a division of the City of Columbus, Ohio, Department of Development. Its mandate: maintain, interpret, and periodically update the city's comprehensive zoning plan. The word "periodically" has been interpreted generously. The current comprehensive plan update has been "in progress" since 2019.

The department occupies the fourth floor of 77 North Front Street. There are eleven full-time staff, four of whom are eligible for retirement. The office still runs a FileMaker Pro database from 2003 that tracks variance requests. Three attempts to migrate the database have failed. The fourth attempt is currently on hold pending a budget allocation that was requested in FY2023 and has not been approved.

Director Margaret Huang has led the department since 2015. She is competent, exhausted, and deeply suspicious of technology vendors who promise to "modernize" her workflows. She has sat through fourteen vendor demos in the last three years. Each vendor promised the same thing: automate the zoning review process. Each vendor's demo broke when she asked it to handle a conditional use permit with three variances and an overlay district. She stopped attending demos in 2024.

## How This RFP Reached Us

A junior planner named Devon Akinyemi found the `but-ai` RFP on Hacker News during a lunch break. Devon is 27, has a master's in urban planning from Ohio State, and spends approximately 40% of his working time manually cross-referencing zoning code sections because the department's lookup system does not support full-text search.

Devon convinced Margaret to let him assemble a small team to respond to the RFP. Margaret agreed on three conditions: (1) it must not interfere with existing workflows, (2) it must not require new hardware purchases, and (3) Devon must still complete his regular caseload. Devon agreed to all three and immediately regretted condition three.

## Philosophy

The department does not have a philosophy. It has procedures. The procedures are documented in a 340-page manual last revised in 2016. The manual specifies everything from the format of variance request cover sheets (Letter size, 1-inch margins, 12-point Times New Roman) to the order in which zoning districts are listed in reports (alphabetical by district code, except overlay districts, which are listed after base districts).

If pressed, Margaret would say the department's philosophy is: do it right, do it completely, and document everything. She would also say that "innovation" is a word used by people who have never had to explain to a City Council member why a zoning decision was reversed on appeal.

## Tension

**The Modernization Standoff.** Devon believes the department should adopt AI tools to accelerate zoning review and reduce the backlog of 847 pending variance requests. Dolores believes that speed is the enemy of accuracy and that the backlog exists because the City Council keeps approving developments that require variances instead of updating the zoning code. They are both right. Margaret mediates by allowing Devon to experiment with AI tools on a subset of cases while Dolores continues processing the backlog manually. Neither is satisfied with this arrangement, but both respect Margaret enough to comply.

## Achievement

In 2022, the department completed a comprehensive rezoning of the Franklinton neighborhood — the first neighborhood-scale rezoning in Columbus in fifteen years. The rezoning took three years, involved 47 public meetings, processed 1,200 public comments, and produced a 280-page final document. The plan was adopted unanimously by City Council. Dolores considers it the finest work of her career. Devon considers it evidence that the process needs to be faster. Margaret considers it proof that the process works exactly as it should, even when it is slow.
