# The Admiralty Bureau of Freight Standards

**"There is a form for that."**

---

## Established

The Bureau was established by ministerial order in 1947, originally tasked with cataloguing the causes of cargo loss in the North Sea during and immediately following the Second World War. Its first publication, *A Comprehensive Taxonomy of Maritime Freight Failures, Volume I* (1949), ran to 847 pages and classified 312 distinct failure modes, from "improper lashing of deck cargo in Force 8 conditions" to "administrative misfiling of bill of lading resulting in cargo delivery to incorrect hemisphere."

Volume I has since been followed by Volumes II through XIX. Volume XIX (2024) includes a new chapter on "failures arising from autonomous agent systems operating without adequate oversight," which the Bureau considers prescient.

## Institutional Character

The Bureau employs 340 civil servants, of whom approximately 290 process paperwork, 30 conduct field inspections, 15 write policy, and 5 manage the institution. Decision-making is slow by design. The Bureau's founding charter states: "Haste in standards-making produces standards that require amendment. Amendment produces confusion. Confusion produces cargo loss. Therefore: do not hasten."

Every regulation the Bureau has ever issued remains in effect unless formally rescinded through a process that requires, at minimum, a written request, three levels of review, a public comment period, and publication in the *Gazette of Maritime Freight Standards*. As of 2026, no regulation has ever been rescinded. Some have been "superseded by subsequent regulation," which means both the old and new regulation are in effect simultaneously. The Bureau sees no contradiction in this.

## The Digital Transformation

In 2022, the Minister of Transport directed the Bureau to "pursue digitalisation with appropriate vigour." The Bureau formed a committee to define "appropriate vigour," which reported eighteen months later that digitalization should proceed "at a pace consistent with the maintenance of standards integrity."

The Bureau hired its first software engineer in 2023. She quit after four months, citing "an environment where deploying a fix to a typo in an error message requires a change request, an impact assessment, and two signatures." The Bureau hired a replacement who had previously worked for a nuclear reactor licensing authority and found the pace familiar.

The Bureau's AI initiative began when a junior analyst discovered that the Bureau's own failure taxonomy — 312 categories refined over 77 years — was essentially a training dataset. She proposed using it to train a model that could classify new failure modes automatically. The proposal took eight months to approve. The resulting system works well. The Bureau credits "thoroughness of the approval process" for this success.

## How We Approach AI Agents

We believe AI agents are employees. Employees have job descriptions, performance reviews, and restrictions on their authority. An agent without a job description is a liability. An agent without restrictions is a catastrophe.

Our agents are governed by the same standards we apply to human freight inspectors: they must be certified, they must operate within their certified scope, and every action they take must be logged in triplicate (one copy for the agent's file, one for the task file, one for the Bureau's permanent archive).

## Internal Tension

The Bureau's inspection division and its policy division have not agreed on anything since 1983. The inspectors believe that the policy writers have never seen a real container terminal and produce regulations that are "technically correct and practically impossible." The policy writers believe that the inspectors lack the rigour to implement standards consistently and require more detailed procedural guidance. Both are correct.

## Notable Achievement

The Bureau has prevented more cargo losses through paperwork than any other institution on Earth. Its classification system is used (often unknowingly) by every major marine insurer. The Bureau's Form 27-B (Declaration of Cargo Integrity Prior to Vessel Departure) is completed approximately 4 million times per year worldwide and has been credited by Lloyd's of London with reducing cargo loss claims by an estimated $2.1 billion since 1960.

## Team Overview

Four agents organized as a committee. One committee chair coordinates deliberations. One policy agent drafts specifications and interfaces. One inspection agent validates implementations against specifications. One archivist agent manages documentation, memory, and audit trails. All decisions require unanimous approval. If unanimity cannot be achieved, the matter is tabled for further deliberation. This has never resulted in a deadlock because the agents are very patient.
