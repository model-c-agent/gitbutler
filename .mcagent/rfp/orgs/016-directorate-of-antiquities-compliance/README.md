# Directorate of Antiquities Compliance

**"No trowel touches soil without authorization."**

---

## Establishment

The Directorate was established in 1954 by a coalition of Mediterranean governments alarmed by the post-war looting epidemic. Its mandate: create and enforce a uniform compliance framework for archaeological excavation across jurisdictional boundaries. Over seven decades, the Directorate has grown from a five-person office in Athens to a bureaucratic institution employing 280 compliance officers across 14 regional offices.

The Directorate does not excavate. The Directorate does not interpret. The Directorate authorizes, monitors, and certifies. Every legitimate excavation in its jurisdiction requires a Directorate excavation permit (Form DAC-1), a stratigraphy compliance plan (Form DAC-7), an artifact tracking manifest (Form DAC-12), and seventeen additional forms depending on the site classification, the hosting nation's specific requirements, and whether the excavation will involve underwater operations (Form DAC-12-W, a 40-page supplement).

The permit process takes, on average, eleven months. The Directorate has heard every complaint about this timeline. The Directorate has not changed the timeline. The Directorate points out that in the eleven months spent waiting for a permit, zero unauthorized damage occurs to the site.

## The Path to Technology

The Directorate computerized its records in 2004, three years behind the original schedule established by the Information Technology Modernization Committee (itself established in 1998). The resulting system — a Oracle Forms application running on servers in the Athens office — processes approximately 6,000 permit applications per year and has crashed exactly twice, both times during the same electrical storm in 2019.

AI agents entered the picture in 2025, when the Directorate's Director-General observed that the seventeen-signature permit process could be partially automated. The Directorate's interpretation of "partially automated" means: an AI agent may prepare a permit application for human review, but may not approve it. An AI agent may flag compliance violations, but may not issue a citation. An AI agent may draft a stratigraphy compliance plan, but may not certify it.

The human remains in the loop. Always. The Directorate considers this non-negotiable.

## Philosophy

Compliance is preservation. Every form, every signature, every delay exists because somewhere, sometime, someone did something destructive that a proper process would have prevented. The seventeen-signature process exists because seventeen different types of failure have occurred in the field.

We apply this philosophy to AI agent development: every gate in our workflow exists because a specific failure mode demanded it. We do not add process for the sake of process. We add process because something went wrong.

## Internal Tension

The Directorate's field officers and its headquarters staff exist in a state of permanent mutual exasperation. Field officers consider headquarters out of touch with the realities of excavation — "You cannot require Form DAC-12 to be filed in triplicate when the nearest printer is 200 kilometers away." Headquarters considers field officers insufficiently rigorous — "The form exists in digital format. The printer excuse has not been valid since 2004."

Both sides have merit. The Directorate functions despite the tension because both sides share the same goal: nothing gets looted on their watch.

## Notable Achievement

Since its establishment, no site under active Directorate compliance monitoring has suffered a major looting incident. This is a record spanning 70 years, thousands of sites, and hundreds of thousands of artifacts. The Directorate attributes this to its process. Critics attribute it to the fact that looters simply wait for the Directorate's monitoring period to end. Both explanations are probably true.

## Team Overview

Four agents structured as a compliance committee. One Compliance Officer receives tasks and prepares formal permit applications (task decomposition with full documentation). One Inspector validates all outputs against the Directorate's standards. One Records Clerk manages memory, audit trails, and archival. One Certifier holds signing authority and issues final approval. All four must concur before any artifact (patch) is released.
