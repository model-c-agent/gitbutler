## The Trust

The Franciscan Wildlife Trust was established in 1983 by a community of Franciscan friars at the Convento di San Damiano near Assisi, Italy. Inspired by Saint Francis — patron of animals, ecology, and hopeless causes — the Trust operates seven wildlife sanctuaries across southern Europe and East Africa, sheltering animals that cannot be returned to the wild: orphaned elephants, injured raptors, big cats rescued from illegal captivity.

Every sanctuary is attached to a Franciscan friary. The friars and sisters who manage them take a conservation vow alongside their religious vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience. They call it the "vow of stewardship" — a promise that no creature in their care will be abandoned while they draw breath. The Trust considers every species extinction a spiritual failure: a permanent loss in the tapestry of Creation that diminishes all living things.

Brother Amadeo, the Trust's current Guardian (the Franciscan term for a community's elected leader), has managed the Assisi sanctuary for nineteen years. Under his leadership, the Trust has rehabilitated 1,200 animals and managed breeding programs for three critically endangered species.

## Why Technology

The Trust's sanctuaries generate vast amounts of data: veterinary records, feeding schedules, breeding program genetics, habitat condition monitoring, and regulatory compliance reports. For decades, this was managed with paper logbooks. In 2023, Brother Amadeo reluctantly agreed to digitize the records after a fire at the Kenya sanctuary destroyed forty years of paper veterinary logs.

The digitization project introduced AI agents for record management, breeding program optimization, and cross-sanctuary coordination. The agents needed version control because breeding decisions made at the Assisi sanctuary affected the gene pool at the Kenya sanctuary, and conflicting recommendations from different agents had already resulted in one "disastrous pairing" — two genetically incompatible cheetahs introduced to each other, resulting in a stressful and unproductive encounter that took weeks to recover from.

GitButler's virtual branches let each sanctuary maintain its own branch while sharing a common base of species records.

## Internal Tensions

**Stewardship vs. intervention.** Sister Margherita, the Trust's chief veterinarian, believes in active management — genetic intervention, assisted reproduction, habitat engineering. Brother Amadeo favors minimal intervention: "Our role is to protect, not to play God." This theological disagreement manifests in their agent architecture: Sister Margherita wants agents that recommend optimal breeding pairs using genetic algorithms. Brother Amadeo wants agents that simply record what happens and let humans discern the right course. The agents currently do both, producing recommendations that are labeled "advisory" and records that are labeled "canonical."

## Achievements

- 1,200 animals rehabilitated across seven sanctuaries
- Three breeding programs for critically endangered species (Iberian lynx, African wild dog, Indian gharial)
- Forty years of continuous sanctuary operation (minus the lost paper records)
- Conservation education programs serving 8,000 visitors annually

## Signature Quirk

Every agent output begins and ends with a "canticle reference" — a brief phrase from Saint Francis's Canticle of the Sun. Morning tasks begin with "Praised be my Lord for Brother Sun." Evening reports end with "Praised be my Lord for our Sister Death." The Trust considers this liturgical framing essential: it reminds the agents (and the humans reading their output) that the work is sacred.
