# Olfactory Precision Unit

**"Fragrance formulation is a discipline. Treat it like one."**

## Unit History

The Olfactory Precision Unit (OPU) was established in 2019 as a subdivision of DARPA's Materials and Biological Technology Office. The original mandate was narrow: develop a computational framework for predicting olfactory signatures of chemical compounds, to assist in identifying novel chemical agents by their smell. The project was classified. The results were not.

The unit discovered that their computational framework was better at designing fragrances than at detecting threats. The olfactory prediction model, trained on 140,000 molecular structures and their documented scent profiles, could generate novel molecules with specified olfactory properties — "design a molecule that smells like fresh linen and wet stone" — with 73% accuracy on blind evaluation. The defense application fizzled. The fragrance application exploded.

In 2021, the unit was partially declassified and reorganized as a dual-use research group operating from a lab at the Edgewood Chemical Biological Center in Aberdeen Proving Ground, Maryland. The lab looks like what you would expect: stainless steel surfaces, fume hoods, gas chromatography-mass spectrometry rigs, and a vaguely unsettling number of locked cabinets. There are also 400 small brown glass bottles on a rack, labeled by CAS number, containing the unit's reference library of aroma chemicals.

Colonel Sarah Oduya commands the unit. She is an Army chemical officer who pivoted to materials science after two tours and holds a PhD in computational chemistry from Georgia Tech. She runs the unit like a research lab embedded in a military structure: disciplined schedules, rigorous protocols, and a surprising tolerance for creative dissent during the "formulation hypothesis" phase of each project.

## Tension

**The Dual-Use Dilemma.** Dr. Tanaka wants to publish the unit's olfactory prediction model as open research. Colonel Oduya supports publication in principle but must navigate the classification review process, which takes 6-18 months per publication and has rejected three of the unit's last five papers on grounds of "potential dual-use concern." Tanaka finds this stifling. Oduya finds it non-negotiable. They respect each other enormously and disagree about this constantly.

## Achievement

In 2023, the unit's olfactory prediction model identified a novel musk molecule — designated OPU-M17 — that was subsequently synthesized, evaluated by a panel of ten professional perfumers, and rated as "exceptional: a warm, clean musk with unusual persistence and no detectable animalic undertone." OPU-M17 was licensed (non-exclusively) to three fragrance houses. It is the first synthetic musk molecule discovered entirely by computational prediction. Dr. Tanaka keeps the original GC-MS chromatogram on his office wall. Sgt. Reyes keeps a 10ml sample in her desk drawer and occasionally opens it when she needs to remember why the unit exists.
