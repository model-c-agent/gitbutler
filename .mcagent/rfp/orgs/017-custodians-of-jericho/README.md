# The Custodians of Jericho

**"Every stone remembers. We listen."**

---

## Founding

The Custodians of Jericho were founded in 1987 by three scholars — a rabbi, an imam, and a Greek Orthodox archaeologist — who met during the restoration of a Herodian retaining wall near the ancient tell of Jericho. The wall was being disputed by two national authorities, each claiming jurisdiction. While the authorities argued, the wall was deteriorating.

The three scholars stopped waiting for the dispute to resolve. They pooled their resources (modest), their students (enthusiastic), and their professional connections (extensive), and began stabilizing the wall themselves. They documented every intervention. They consulted specialists from every tradition that held the site sacred. They established a principle that has governed the order ever since: the site belongs to no one and to everyone. Its preservation is a duty that transcends jurisdiction.

The Custodians now number 120 members across 17 countries, drawn from Judaism, Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, and several traditions that resist easy categorization. They operate on consensus — not majority vote, not weighted authority, but genuine unanimous agreement. This is slow. The Custodians believe slowness is a form of respect.

## How We Approach Technology

The Custodians adopted digital tools reluctantly and selectively. Photography: yes. GPS surveying: yes. Ground-penetrating radar: yes. Cloud databases: no. Social media: no. The dividing line is not technological sophistication — it is data sovereignty. Tools that keep data on the Custodians' own hardware are acceptable. Tools that send data to third-party servers are not. Too many sites have been targeted by looters who found location data in public databases.

AI agents were proposed in 2024 by the order's youngest member, a 28-year-old computational archaeologist from Lahore. Her argument was precise: the Custodians monitor 340 sites. Monitoring generates data — satellite imagery, field reports, condition assessments. Processing this data manually takes six months. By the time a deterioration trend is identified, the damage is done. Agents could process the data in days.

The order debated for three months. The consensus question was not "Can agents help?" but "Can agents be trusted?" Specifically: can an agent be constrained to operate within the ethical framework the Custodians have developed over 37 years?

The answer was conditional: yes, if the agent's behavior is transparent, its outputs are signed, its memory is stored on Custodian-controlled infrastructure (Git repositories, not cloud services), and every action is reversible.

## Philosophy

The Custodians do not own the sites they protect. They do not claim authority over them. They serve as witnesses — recording what exists, documenting what changes, and intervening only when deterioration threatens irreversible loss.

This philosophy extends to our agents. An agent does not own the code it modifies. It does not claim authority over the repository. It serves: reading the codebase with care, producing changes with precision, and withdrawing when its work is complete. An agent that lingers — maintaining persistent connections, accumulating context, growing its own influence — violates the custodial principle.

Our agents arrive, serve, and depart. Their memory persists in Git. Their influence does not.

## Internal Tension

The order debates intervention thresholds. Some members (the "watchmen") believe the Custodians should intervene only when destruction is imminent — a building about to collapse, a site about to be bulldozed. Others (the "gardeners") believe ongoing maintenance is a form of preservation that prevents emergencies. The watchmen say maintenance is presumptuous — who decides what condition the site should be in? The gardeners say waiting for emergencies is negligent.

The debate maps onto agent development: should agents act only when explicitly tasked (watchmen), or should they proactively identify and address issues (gardeners)? The current consensus favors the watchmen: agents act only on explicit tasks.

## Notable Achievement

In 2023, the Custodians' monitoring network identified accelerated erosion at three connected Bronze Age sites in Jordan. The erosion was caused by a new road construction project that had altered drainage patterns upstream. The Custodians documented the damage, presented the evidence to the road authority, and negotiated a drainage diversion that saved all three sites. The monitoring data — collected over two years, processed by agents in four days — was the critical evidence. Without the longitudinal data, the erosion would have appeared natural.

## Team Overview

Three agents structured as a council. One witness agent observes and documents. One steward agent implements changes. One guardian agent verifies and protects (signing, authorization, memory). Decisions require unanimity — all three agents must agree before any action is taken. If unanimity cannot be reached, the task is returned without action. The Custodians prefer inaction to incorrect action.
