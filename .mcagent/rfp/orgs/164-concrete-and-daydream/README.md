# Concrete & Daydream

**"A city is a poem that no one finishes writing."**

---

## How It Started

Concrete & Daydream is not really an organization. It is a residency. It is twenty-three people at any given time, living and working in a converted bus depot in Marseille, making things about cities. Some of them are architects. Some are muralists. Some are zoning lawyers who got disillusioned and picked up a camera instead. One is a former civil engineer who now makes kinetic sculptures out of rebar.

The residency was founded in 2016 by Yara Khoury and Jean-Marc Belliveau, who met during a public charrette for the redesign of the Vieux-Port promenade. Yara was there representing a neighborhood association. Jean-Marc was there representing the design firm hired by the city. They disagreed about everything — the width of the bike lane, the placement of the bollards, the species of tree — and fell in love over the disagreement.

They married in 2017, divorced in 2019, and remained creative partners. The bus depot was their divorce settlement. Neither wanted to sell it, so they turned it into a residency for people who think about cities the way they do: as living, contested, beautiful, unjust, and perpetually unfinished things.

## Philosophy

Art is how cities think about themselves. A mural on a wall changes how people move through a block. A zoning map is a drawing that determines who gets to live where. Concrete & Daydream does not distinguish between art and planning — it treats both as acts of spatial imagination.

We do not have a formal methodology. We have a single rule: every project must be rooted in a specific place. No abstractions. No "the generic urban condition." You pick a block, a corner, a bus stop. You spend time there. You make something about it. The something can be a painting, a policy brief, an interactive map, or a short film. The medium does not matter. The specificity does.

## Why We Are Here

In 2024, three residents — Lior, Nadia, and Kwame — started using AI agents to generate 3D models of public spaces from community descriptions. The idea was radical: instead of architects designing a park and then asking the community for feedback, the community would describe the park they wanted and agents would visualize it in real time.

The agents needed to collaborate. One agent parsed natural language descriptions. Another generated geometry. A third applied urban design constraints (setbacks, sight lines, ADA compliance). They produced outputs as patches to a shared spatial model. The patches conflicted constantly. Nadia started using GitButler to manage the conflicts and realized the virtual branch model was the first version control system that matched the residency's creative process: everyone working on the same place simultaneously, from different perspectives, with reconciliation deferred until someone was ready to merge.

## Tension

**The Accessibility Argument.** Kwame insists that all agent outputs — patches, commit messages, coordination messages — must be legible to non-technical community members. He wants commit messages in plain language: "Added a bench facing south near the old fountain" instead of "feat(park-model): add seating node at coords [43.2965, 5.3698]." Lior argues that technical precision is essential for reproducibility and that dumbing down commit messages sacrifices information. They compromise differently on every project, which means there is no consistent commit message style across the residency's repositories. This drives Nadia, who values consistency, to quiet despair.

## Notable Achievement

In 2025, the Marseille municipal government commissioned Concrete & Daydream to facilitate a participatory design process for three public spaces in the 3rd arrondissement, one of the city's poorest neighborhoods. The residency deployed AI agents in a community center, where residents spoke in French, Arabic, and Comorian about the spaces they wanted. The agents generated real-time spatial models projected on a wall. Over three evenings, 200 residents contributed to designs that were formally adopted into the city's master plan. It was the first time AI-mediated participatory design was included in an official French planning document.
