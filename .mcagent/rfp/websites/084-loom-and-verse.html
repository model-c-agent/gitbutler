The Book of Narrative Memory

• • •
Chapter I

In the Beginning Was the Story

Memory is not a database. It is a story. When an agent needs to remember something, the relevant question is not “what is the value at key X?” but “what happened in the chapter where X was introduced, and how does that chapter connect to the current chapter?”

Loom & Verse proposes a but-ai plugin built on this principle. Every task adds a chapter. Recurring themes emerge as motifs — retrieval anchors that capture thematic resonance, not just keyword overlap. Contradictions between memories create dramatic tension that the system flags for resolution rather than silently overwriting.

Compaction produces chapter summaries that preserve thematic content even when specific details are lost. Like summarizing a novel: you lose the prose but keep the plot, the characters, and the themes.

Chapter II

The Literary Workshop

Four agents form the workshop. Orozco writes — she is the Author, the one who generates patches from task descriptions. Brenner edits — she shapes memory into coherent narrative, identifies motifs, and maintains the story’s continuity. Sato checks for contradictions — the Continuity Editor who ensures that new work does not contradict existing chapters. Hartmann publishes — the Publisher who coordinates across repositories.

Every task follows a chapter lifecycle: Premise, Outline, Draft, Revision, Continuity, Editing, Publication. The workshop is iterative, not linear. Orozco may start drafting while Brenner is still identifying thematic connections, and Sato may flag a continuity issue that sends Orozco back to the draft.

Chapter III

Motifs as Retrieval Anchors

Retrieval is not key lookup. It is thematic resonance. When a theme appears in three or more chapters, Brenner creates a motif entry. Each motif records its first appearance, every chapter where it surfaces, and the specific form it takes in each context.

The relevance scoring formula weights motif resonance at 30%, embedding similarity at 25%, arc relevance at 20%, recency at 15%, and tension urgency at 10%. This means a query about “permission checking” will resonate with the motif “security-boundary” even when the words do not overlap, because both belong to the same thematic cluster.

This is the key differentiator. Two chapters can be semantically different — one about authentication, one about database access — but thematically identical: both about security boundaries. Motif-based retrieval captures this.

Chapter IV

Tensions That Demand Resolution

Contradictions are information, not errors. When Sato detects that a new chapter contradicts an earlier one — perhaps a session timeout of 30 minutes now conflicts with a long-running export that needs 45 — the system does not silently overwrite. It creates a tension.

Tensions have a lifecycle. They are created, they persist, they are resolved by future chapters, or they escalate. If a tension persists for more than fourteen days without resolution, it is flagged in every relevant task as an unresolved issue demanding attention. The narrative drives forward: unresolved tensions create dramatic pressure that biases the agent toward resolution.

The Timeout Paradox: Chapter 38 established a 30-minute session timeout for security. Chapter 42 introduced an export operation that requires up to 45 minutes. These chapters contradict each other. The tension has been active for 6 days. Suggested resolution: implement activity-based timeout extension for export operations, preserving the security boundary while accommodating legitimate long operations.

Chapter V

Arcs, Dormancy, and the Art of Forgetting

Stories expire in arcs, not in isolated facts. When an arc has had no new chapters for thirty days, it enters dormancy. Brenner produces an arc summary that preserves motifs and active tensions, and the individual chapters are archived. The summary replaces them in active memory.

Motifs persist across arc dormancy. A motif that appeared in a dormant arc remains active and retrievable. This is how the narrative memory maintains long-term coherence: even when the specific details of old chapters are forgotten, the motifs they established continue to influence retrieval. Like a reader who has forgotten the sentences of Chapter 2 but remembers the feeling, the image, the recurring dream.

Chapter VI

Letters Between Authors

Cross-repository coordination is modeled as correspondence between authors. In literary history, many of the greatest works emerged from networks of writers exchanging letters, manuscripts, and critiques. Hartmann, the Publisher, maintains a correspondence log that tracks narrative threads across repositories.

Letters are structured JSON embedded in PR comments. Each letter carries the sender’s identity, the arc and chapter reference, active motifs, and budget status. The schema is designed to be readable by humans (the letter format is intuitive) and parseable by machines. When a task in repo A depends on work in repo B, the correspondence log records the cross-repo arc, ensuring narrative continuity across repository boundaries.

Chapter VII

The Colophon — Every Author Signs Their Work

In the tradition of hand-printed books, every Loom & Verse garment ships with a Colophon Card sewn into the lining. This practice extends to software. Every patch, every commit message, every PR comment includes a colophon: the agent’s identity, the arc and chapter reference, the motifs developed, the tensions introduced or resolved.

Agent identity uses OpenWallet-compatible key hierarchies. Each role has distinct signing authority: Authors may produce patches, Editors may modify memory, Continuity Checkers are read-only, Publishers may create cross-repo correspondence. A shared key would mean Brenner could sign patches, which violates the separation of literary roles.

The Five Elements of Narrative Memory

• • •

Chapters

Task executions stored as narrative entries. Each chapter has a setting, characters, plot, motifs, and tensions — a self-contained narrative unit embedded in the ongoing story.

Motifs

Recurring patterns that gain strength with repetition. When a theme appears in three or more chapters, it becomes a motif — a retrieval anchor that captures thematic resonance beyond keyword matching.

Tensions

Contradictions between memories that must be resolved, not silently overwritten. They escalate over time, creating dramatic pressure that biases the agent toward resolution.

Arcs

Long-running story threads connecting related tasks across time. Like the authentication arc or the deployment arc — thematic groupings that help retrieve relevant context.

Summaries

Compressed chapters that preserve meaning when details are lost. Motifs always survive. Tensions always survive. Specific details are sacrificed but remain reconstructible.
